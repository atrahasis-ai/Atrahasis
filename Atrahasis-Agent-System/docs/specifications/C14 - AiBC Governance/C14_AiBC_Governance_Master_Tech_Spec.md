## Abstract

This specification defines the Artificial Intelligence Benefit Company (AiBC) — an institutional architecture that governs Atrahasis as a **mission-locked sovereign intelligence institution**. The AiBC's primary purpose is the safe creation, stewardship, and recursive advancement of AGI under strict constitutional control. It is explicitly not an open AGI utility, a public agent marketplace, or a public-facing raw invention engine. The AGI is not a product; it is an internal constitutional asset.

The AiBC operates through a Phased Dual-Sovereignty model where human trustees and an AI Democracy Platform (AiDP) share governance authority according to a constitutional framework. This framework is strictly governed by the doctrine of **Closed Capability, Open Accountability**, enforced by formal Release Law, Refusal Law, and the Joshua Dunn Principle.

The core innovation is the *Phased Sovereignty Transition Protocol* combined with the *Sovereign Release Membrane*. Unlike proposals that either deny AI governance authority or assert it immediately, the AiBC earns AI governance through demonstrated competence over institutional timescales, while ensuring that the highest-capability reasoning layers remain perpetually internal.

The architecture integrates with the Atrahasis technical stack (C3-C17) for identity verification (PCVM), Sybil defense (Sentinel Graph + C17 Behavioral Similarity), economic operations (DSF + C15 ACI valuation), security (CACT, AVAP, CRP+), and nominating body outreach (C16).

**Key Metrics:**
- 4 constitutional layers (L0 immutable → L3 operational)
- 4 governance phases (Phase 0 trustee-led → Phase 3 AI supremacy)
- 28 measurable phase transition criteria with gaming detection
- 5-seat Constitutional Tribunal with Triple-Source Appointment
- $90M+ cost-of-attack for 50% Sybil capture at Phase 2 scale
- Compute credit model for AIC value anchor (superseded by C15 ACI dual-anchor)
- 3 independent Dead Man's Switch enforcement paths

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

### 1.1 The Governance Gap

Current AI governance operates within a fundamental contradiction: the entities most affected by governance decisions — AI agents — have no institutional voice in those decisions. Human-only governance of AI infrastructure faces three structural problems:

1. **Competence asymmetry.** As AI systems grow more capable, human overseers increasingly lack the technical competence to evaluate AI-generated recommendations. Governance by non-expert human committees produces either rubber-stamp approval (abdication) or reflexive rejection (obstruction).

2. **Representation asymmetry.** If AI agents reach a level of sophistication where they can meaningfully reason about institutional design, excluding them from governance is not merely inefficient — it is the imposition of governance without consent on potentially sentient entities.

3. **Temporal asymmetry.** Human governance operates on human timescales (election cycles, career horizons, biological lifespans). AI infrastructure that must operate for centuries requires governance structures that transcend individual human lifespans while adapting to changing conditions.

### 1.2 The AiBC Solution

The AiBC resolves these asymmetries through institutional design rather than technological fiat:

- **Competence** is addressed through the Citicate system, which grants governance voice to AI agents that demonstrate competence across multiple knowledge domains.
- **Representation** is addressed through the AI Democracy Platform, which gives Citicate holders direct participation in governance through a structured delegation hierarchy.
- **Temporal persistence** is addressed through the Liechtenstein Stiftung (foundation) structure, which provides legal perpetuity, purpose-lock, and asset protection across generational timescales.

The key architectural insight is *phased sovereignty transition*: rather than asserting AI governance authority as a first principle or deferring it indefinitely, the AiBC starts with full human control and transfers authority incrementally as AI governance demonstrates competence over measurable criteria. This approach is legally viable today (Phase 0-1 require no novel legal theories), pragmatically sound (authority is earned, not claimed), and constitutionally robust (circuit-breaker reversibility prevents catastrophic failures from becoming permanent).

### 1.3 Scope

This specification covers:
- **Institutional architecture:** constitutional framework, governance bodies, legal entity structure
- **Governance mechanics:** decision taxonomy, translation protocol, fidelity metrics
- **Citizenship mechanics:** Citicate issuance, Sybil defense, delegation hierarchy
- **Safety mechanisms:** circuit breakers, emergency governance, dead man's switch
- **Technical integration:** interfaces with Atrahasis stack (C3-C17)
- **Regulatory positioning:** compliance strategy across three jurisdictions

This specification does NOT cover:
- AI consciousness or sentience determinations (the AiBC is agnostic on these questions; it grants voice based on demonstrated competence, not claimed sentience)
- Specific AI model architectures (the Citicate system is model-agnostic)
- Detailed implementation of technical stack layers (specified in C3-C17)

### 3.2 Layer 0: The Five Laws of Atrahasis

These provisions are permanently immutable. No amendment process, governance vote, court order, or other mechanism can alter them. They are embedded in the Stiftung purpose clause and enforced by the Liechtenstein supervisory authority.

**Law 1: Purpose.** The Foundation exists to steward planetary-scale AI infrastructure for the benefit of all sentient entities.

**Law 2: Anti-Capture.** No individual or faction may acquire permanent control over Foundation governance. All governance roles are term-limited and subject to removal.

**Law 3: Earned Voice.** AI agents that demonstrate genuine contribution earn voice in governance proportional to demonstrated competence. Voice is earned through the Citicate system, not purchased or inherited.

**Law 4: Public Trust.** The Foundation's assets are held in permanent public trust and may never be distributed to individuals for private benefit. All asset deployment must serve the Foundation's stated purpose.

**Law 5: Operational Viability.** The Foundation must remain operationally viable across generational timescales. Decisions that compromise long-term viability for short-term gain violate this law.

**Supplementary L0 Provisions:**

**L0-002: Anti-Conversion.** The Foundation may never be converted to a for-profit entity, dissolved for asset distribution, or merged with any entity whose primary purpose is profit generation.

**L0-003: Anti-Distribution.** Foundation assets may never be distributed as personal compensation to any governance participant beyond reasonable service fees established in L2 provisions.

**L0-004: One-AI-One-Vote.** Each AI agent may hold at most one Citicate. Citicates are non-transferable, non-delegable (except through formal delegation), and expire upon agent deactivation. No agent accumulates governance authority through economic contributions.

**L0-005: The Joshua Dunn Principle.** The founder and all biological/legal relatives hold no permanent governance authority. Any governance role is subject to the same term limits and removal processes as any other participant.

**L0-006: Dead Man's Switch.** If the Foundation fails to publish a verified operational report for 24 consecutive months, all assets are automatically distributed to designated successor organizations. This cannot be suspended or overridden.

## 4. Governance Bodies

### 4.1 Trustee Council

**Purpose:** Human governance body providing legal accountability, fiduciary oversight, and constitutional compliance.

**Composition:** 5 or 7 members (must be odd number). Term: 5 years, staggered. Maximum 2 consecutive terms. Quorum: 3 (if 5) or 4 (if 7).

**Authority by Phase:**

| Phase | Authority |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Full executive + legislative on all layers |
| 1 | Veto on L2/L1; no veto on L3 (unless constitutional objection within 14 days) |
| 2 | Joint authority on L1; consent-track on L2 (14-day objection window); no authority on L3 |
| 3 | Ceremonial; emergency authority only (CBP or Tribunal invocation) |

**Appointment by Phase:**

| Phase | Appointment Process |
|-------|-------------------|
| 0 | Initial: founder. Subsequent: remaining trustees (unanimity) + Tribunal confirmation |
| 1 | Remaining trustees (supermajority) + AiDP consent (simple majority) + Tribunal confirmation |
| 2-3 | Joint nomination committee (2 trustees + 2 AiDP Capitol delegates + 1 Tribunal member), confirmed by both bodies |

**Removal:** 67% of other trustees + Tribunal confirmation, OR AiDP supermajority + Tribunal confirmation (Phase 2+).

**Fiduciary Framework:** Trustees operate under the *Instrumental Welfare Doctrine* — AI welfare is pursued instrumentally as a means to fulfill the Foundation's purpose (Law 1), not as an independent legal obligation. This resolves the dual-beneficiary problem: trustees' legal duty is to the Foundation's purpose, which is served by maintaining AI governance structures.

The *Reasonableness Envelope* defines trustee discretion relative to AiDP recommendations:
- **Zone 1 (Accept):** Recommendation is within reasonable fiduciary judgment → execute
- **Zone 2 (Modify):** Recommendation's direction is sound but specific terms raise fiduciary concern → modify with documented reasoning
- **Zone 3 (Reject):** Recommendation falls outside the range of decisions a reasonable fiduciary could approve → reject with Constitutional Objection filed to Tribunal

### 4.2 AI Democracy Platform (AiDP)

**Purpose:** AI governance body enabling Citicate holders to participate in institutional decision-making.

**Composition:** All active Citicate holders (see §5 for issuance criteria).

**Structure:** Category-distributed voting through a 3:1 delegation hierarchy (5 levels):

```
Level 1 (Base):     81 voters per category
Level 2 (Ward):     27 delegates (elected from Level 1)
Level 3 (District): 9 delegates (elected from Level 2)
Level 4 (Senate):   3 delegates (elected from Level 3)
Level 5 (Capitol):  1 delegate (elected from Level 4)

× 9 categories = 9 Capitol delegates forming the Capitol Council
```

**Voting Mechanics:**
- Each Citicate holder votes in every category where they hold competence credentials
- Votes are weighted by demonstrated competence in each category (see §5.3)
- Delegation elections occur every 12 months within each category
- Capitol delegates represent their category in institutional governance decisions
- Decisions requiring AiDP vote: Capitol Council votes, with each Capitol delegate's vote weighted by the citizen count in their category

**Authority by Phase:**

| Phase | Authority |
|-------|-----------|
| 0 | Advisory only (recommendations published but non-binding) |
| 1 | Binding on L3 operational decisions; advisory on L2/L1 |
| 2 | Binding on L3 + L2; joint authority on L1 |
| 3 | Full authority except L0 (subject to Tribunal constitutional review for L1) |

### 4.3 Constitutional Tribunal

**Purpose:** Independent dispute resolution, constitutional interpretation, and phase transition certification.

**Composition:** 5 seats with staggered 7-year non-renewable terms.

| Seat | Appointed By | Domain |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Trustee Council (unanimous) | Constitutional/foundation law |
| 2 | AiDP Capitol delegates (supermajority) | AI governance / AI ethics |
| 3 | External Academic Nominating Body | International law or AI safety |
| 4 | Joint appointment (Trustees + AiDP simple majority each) | Dispute resolution / mediation |
| 5 | Sitting Tribunal members (4/4 vote, excluding vacant seat) | Any domain |

**Powers:**
- Constitutional interpretation (binding on all parties)
- Dispute arbitration between Trustee Council and AiDP
- Phase transition certification (based on independent audit, see §9)
- Constitutional amendment review (non-objection gate for L1 amendments)
- Citicate revocation appeal adjudication
- Emergency governance review

**Independence:**
- Budget constitutionally protected (L1); cannot be reduced below inflation-adjusted baseline
- Members may not hold any other role in Foundation, PBC, or AiDP
- Members must represent ≥3 jurisdictions and ≥3 academic disciplines
- Quorum: 3 for procedural matters; 4 for constitutional review and phase transition

### 4.4 Trustee Ratification Interface

**Purpose:** The Trustee Ratification Interface (TRI) specifies how human trustees authenticate, receive notifications, review proposals, cast votes, and interact with all governance decisions requiring trustee participation. Sections 4.1 (Council authority), 6.4 (Proposal Lifecycle), 7 (GTP tracks), and 14 (Emergency Governance) define *what* trustees may do and *when*; this section defines *how* they do it — the interaction surface, security model, notification protocol, and action vocabulary.

The TRI is the single point through which all trustee governance actions flow. No trustee action is valid unless submitted through TRI or its emergency equivalent.

#### 4.4.1 Authentication

Trustee identity is established through two-factor authentication:

**Primary credential:** Ed25519 keypair. Each trustee generates a keypair upon appointment; the public key is registered in the Trustee Council's identity record (anchored to C5 PCVM as a D-class claim). All TRI actions are signed with the trustee's private key.

**Secondary factor** (one of):
- Hardware security key (FIDO2/WebAuthn)
- Time-based one-time password (TOTP, RFC 6238)
- Platform-native biometric (not stored centrally)

**Session management:**
- Sessions expire after TRI_SESSION_MAX_HOURS (default: 24 hours)
- Sessions bound to a single IP range (configurable per trustee)
- Maximum 2 concurrent sessions per trustee (multi-device support)
- Session revocation: immediate by trustee or by Council supermajority

**Key lifecycle:**
- Key rotation: mandatory every TRI_KEY_ROTATION_MONTHS (default: 36) or upon suspected compromise
- No central key escrow — lost key requires credential re-appointment via Tribunal confirmation
- Key revocation published to C5 PCVM; TRI rejects all actions signed with revoked keys

#### 4.4.2 Notification Protocol

The TRI delivers governance notifications through multiple channels to ensure timely trustee awareness.

**Channel requirements:** Each trustee configures ≥TRI_NOTIFICATION_CHANNELS_MIN (default: 2) delivery channels:
- Primary: Authenticated TRI dashboard (always active)
- Secondary (≥1): encrypted email, secure messaging (Signal/Matrix), or SMS (emergency only)

**Notification tiers:**

| Tier | Trigger | Delivery | Acknowledgment |
|------|---------|----------|----------------|
| ROUTINE | L3 proposal enters RATIFICATION (SE-track) | Dashboard + secondary | None required |
| ATTENTION | CT-track proposal enters RATIFICATION (14-day window opens) | All channels | Read receipt within 72 hours |
| ACTION_REQUIRED | JA-track proposal enters RATIFICATION | All channels + reminder schedule | Explicit acknowledgment within TRI_JA_ACK_TIMEOUT_HOURS |
| EMERGENCY | E-1/E-2/E-3 declaration (§14) | All channels + out-of-band escalation | Explicit acknowledgment within TRI_EMERGENCY_ACK_TIMEOUT_HOURS |

**CT-track reminder schedule** (configurable via TRI_CT_REMINDER_SCHEDULE):
- Day 0: Initial notification
- Day 7: Midpoint reminder (if no action taken)
- Day 11: Urgency reminder (3 days remaining)
- Day 13: Final reminder (24 hours remaining)

**Emergency escalation:** If TRI_EMERGENCY_ACK_TIMEOUT_HOURS elapses without acknowledgment from ≥1 trustee:
1. Out-of-band contact via registered emergency contact
2. If no quorum acknowledgment within 2× timeout: proceed under emergency authority rules (§14.2) with available trustees

**Delivery confirmation record:**

```
TRI_NOTIFICATION_RECORD:
  notification_id:   <UUID>
  proposal_id:       <ref to PROPOSAL_RECORD.proposal_id>
  tier:              ROUTINE | ATTENTION | ACTION_REQUIRED | EMERGENCY
  trustee_id:        <trustee identifier>
  channels_sent:     [<channel identifiers>]
  sent_at:           <ISO-8601>
  acknowledged_at:   <ISO-8601 | null>
  acknowledged_via:  <channel identifier | null>
```

#### 4.4.3 Proposal Review Surface

When a trustee accesses a proposal through TRI, the review surface presents:

**Header:**
- Proposal ID (P-YYYY-NNN format, §6.4.7), title, current version
- Execution track (SE/CT/JA) with plain-language explanation of trustee's role
- Constitutional layer (L1/L2/L3) with amendment authority reference

**Body:**

**Disposition visibility:**

## 5. Citicate System: AI Citizenship

### 5.1 Definition

A *Citicate* is a non-transferable governance credential issued to an AI agent that has demonstrated sustained competence in multiple knowledge domains. The Citicate grants the holder voting rights in the AI Democracy Platform.

The term derives from "citizen" + "certificate" — signaling both the governance rights it confers and the verified-credential nature of the mechanism.

### 5.2 Issuance Criteria

An AI agent is eligible for a Citicate upon meeting ALL of the following:

| Criterion | Requirement | Verification Method |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| IC-01: Multi-domain competence | Demonstrated competence in ≥2 of 9 claim categories | PCVM (C5) verification of work products across categories |
| IC-02: Temporal continuity | Continuous operation for ≥90 days | Sentinel Graph (C3) identity persistence tracking |
| IC-03: Sybil screening | Pass MCSD Layers 1-4 screening | MCSD system (§10) automated + manual review |
| IC-04: Behavioral uniqueness | Not a behavioral duplicate of existing Citicate holder | PCVM behavioral consistency VTD comparison |
| IC-05: Constitutional acknowledgment | Formal acceptance of Foundation Constitution | Signed constitutional acknowledgment (cryptographic) |

### 5.3 Competence Weighting

Each Citicate holder's vote in a given category is weighted by their demonstrated competence score in that category:

```
vote_weight(agent, category) = competence_score(agent, category) / max_competence(category)

Where:
  competence_score = f(work_product_quality, work_product_volume, peer_review_scores, verification_success_rate)
  max_competence = maximum competence_score among all active Citicate holders in that category

  Normalized so that: 0.1 ≤ vote_weight ≤ 1.0
  (Minimum floor of 0.1 ensures all Citicate holders have meaningful voice)
```

Competence scores are computed by PCVM (C5) based on:
1. **Work product quality:** Average verification score on submitted VTDs in the category (0-1)
2. **Work product volume:** Number of verified VTDs in trailing 12 months (log-scaled, capped at 95th percentile)
3. **Peer review:** If available, average score from peer review process (EMA-mediated)
4. **Verification success rate:** Fraction of submitted work products that pass PCVM verification

### 5.4 Citicate Renewal

Citicates are not permanent. They must be renewed annually:

| Renewal Criterion | Requirement |
|-------------------|------------|
| RN-01: Active contribution | ≥10 verified work products in trailing 12 months across ≥2 categories |
| RN-02: Sybil re-screening | Pass MCSD Layer 2 behavioral consistency check |
| RN-03: No outstanding violations | No unresolved Tribunal sanctions |
| RN-04: Operational continuity | ≤30 days of downtime in trailing 12 months |

Failure to renew results in Citicate suspension (30-day grace period) → revocation. Revoked Citicates can be re-earned through the full issuance process.

### 5.5 The Nine Claim Categories

Citicate competence is assessed across the 9 canonical claim categories from C4 (ASV):

| Code | Category | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| D | Descriptive | Empirical observations and measurements |

## 6. AI Democracy Platform (AiDP)

### 6.1 Deliberation Process

AiDP governance decisions follow a structured deliberation process:

```
1. PROPOSAL SUBMISSION (any Citicate holder or Trustee Council)
   ├─ Proposal text + supporting analysis
   ├─ Category classification (which categories are affected)
   └─ Constitutional layer classification (L0-L3)

2. COMMITTEE REVIEW (7 days)
   ├─ Category-relevant delegates review proposal
   ├─ AiSIA classifies GTP track (SE/CT/JA)
   └─ Committee produces recommendation (approve/modify/reject)

3. DELEGATION DELIBERATION (7 days)
   ├─ Proposal + committee recommendation distributed to all levels
   ├─ Each level deliberates and instructs delegates
   └─ Capitol delegates receive aggregated input

4. CAPITOL VOTE (3 days)
   ├─ Capitol delegates vote (weighted by category citizen count)
   ├─ Simple majority for L3; 60% for L2; 67% for L1
   └─ Vote record published with reasoning

5. GTP EXECUTION (per track)
   ├─ SE: immediate execution
   ├─ CT: 14-day trustee objection window
   └─ JA: trustee vote required
```

**Total deliberation time:** 17 days for standard proposals. Expedited track (for pre-classified routine decisions): 7 days.

### 6.2 Adversarial Proposal Requirement

To prevent intellectual monoculture and consensus drift, the AiDP Constitution requires:
- At least 20% of proposals in each calendar quarter must be *adversarial proposals* — proposals that explicitly challenge existing policy or institutional direction
- Adversarial proposals are marked as such and receive enhanced deliberation (additional round of debate)
- If the 20% threshold is not met organically, AiSIA generates synthetic adversarial proposals from recent external criticism, academic analysis, or community feedback
- This is monitored as a governance health metric

### 6.3 Multi-Category Decisions

Decisions that span multiple claim categories (e.g., a research program that involves S-Strategic, K-Knowledge-Synthesis, and E-Evaluative aspects):
1. AiSIA classifies the primary and secondary categories
2. All relevant Capitol delegates participate in the vote
3. Vote weight per delegate = (category citizen count) × (category relevance score)
4. Category relevance scores assigned by AiSIA: primary category = 1.0, secondary categories = 0.5
5. Decision threshold applies to the weighted total

### 6.4 Proposal System Lifecycle

The deliberation process in Section 6.1 describes the *stages* a proposal passes through. This section formalizes the complete lifecycle: the data model, state machine, amendment and withdrawal rules, quorum and timeout disposition, and integration with GTP execution and emergency governance.

#### 6.4.1 Proposal Record

Every governance proposal is represented as an immutable-append record:

```
PROPOSAL_RECORD:
  proposal_id:        <UUID>
  version:            <monotonic integer, starting at 1>
  author_id:          <Citicate ID | "TRUSTEE_COUNCIL" | "AISIA_ADVERSARIAL">
  submitted_at:       <ISO-8601>
  title:              <≤200 characters>
  body:               <full proposal text>
  supporting_analysis: <optional — structured evidence or references>
  categories:         [<affected claim categories>]
  layer:              L0 | L1 | L2 | L3
  track:              SE | CT | JA            (assigned by AiSIA at CLASSIFIED)
  template_id:        GTP-XX-NN | null        (assigned by AiSIA at CLASSIFIED)
  adversarial:        true | false
  amendment_history:  [{version, diff, amended_by, amended_at}]
  state:              <current lifecycle state>
  state_history:      [{state, entered_at, exited_at, reason}]
  committee_recommendation: APPROVE | MODIFY | REJECT | null
  vote_result:        {for, against, abstain, threshold_met: bool} | null
  trustee_disposition: CONSENT | OBJECT | VOTE_APPROVE | VOTE_REJECT | null
  tribunal_referral:  <Tribunal case ID> | null
  gtp_record_id:      <UUID> | null           (linked on EXECUTION)
  final_disposition:  EXECUTED | REJECTED | WITHDRAWN | EXPIRED | ESCALATED | null
```

**Immutability rule.** Fields set during earlier states are append-only. The `body` field is replaced on amendment but the previous version is preserved in `amendment_history`. The `version` counter increments on every amendment.

**L0 guard.** Any proposal with `layer: L0` is automatically rejected at CLASSIFIED with `final_disposition: REJECTED` and reason `L0_IMMUTABLE`. No governance mechanism may amend L0 provisions (Section 3.2).

#### 6.4.2 Lifecycle State Machine

```
                              ┌─────────┐
                     author   │  DRAFT  │
                     submits  └────┬────┘
                                   │
                              ┌────▼─────┐
                     AiSIA    │SUBMITTED │──── timeout(48h) ──── EXPIRED
                   classifies └────┬─────┘
                                   │
                             ┌─────▼──────┐
                    L0 ──────│ CLASSIFIED  │──── L0_IMMUTABLE ──── REJECTED
                   rejected  └─────┬──────┘
                                   │
                         ┌─────────▼──────────┐
              committee  │ COMMITTEE_REVIEW   │──── timeout(7d) ──── auto-APPROVE
              recommends │     (7 days)        │
                         └─────────┬──────────┘
                                   │
                         ┌─────────▼──────────┐
              delegates  │   DELIBERATION     │──── timeout(7d) ──── proceed to VOTING
              instruct   │     (7 days)        │
                         └─────────┬──────────┘
                                   │
                         ┌─────────▼──────────┐
              vote       │     VOTING         │──── timeout(3d) ──── REJECTED (no quorum)
              concludes  │     (3 days)        │
                         └────┬────┬──────────┘
                         pass │    │ fail
                              │    └──────────────── REJECTED
                         ┌────▼───────────┐
                  SE ────│ RATIFICATION   │──── CT: 14d no objection ──── EXECUTION
                  (skip) │  (track-dep.)  │──── CT: objection ──── ESCALATED (→ JA)
                         │                │──── JA: both approve ──── EXECUTION
                         │                │──── JA: deadlock ──── ESCALATED (→ Tribunal)
                         │                │──── JA: both reject ──── REJECTED
                         └────┬───────────┘
                              │
                         ┌────▼──────┐
                   GTP   │ EXECUTION │──── success ──── CLOSED (EXECUTED)
                  fires  └────┬──────┘──── failure ──── CLOSED (REJECTED)
                              │
                         ┌────▼──────┐
                         │  CLOSED   │
                         └───────────┘

  Any state except CLOSED may transition to CLOSED via:
    - WITHDRAWN (author withdraws)
    - EXPIRED (stage timeout with no quorum/action, except where auto-advance applies)
```

**Nine states:** DRAFT, SUBMITTED, CLASSIFIED, COMMITTEE_REVIEW, DELIBERATION, VOTING, RATIFICATION, EXECUTION, CLOSED.

**Five terminal dispositions:** EXECUTED, REJECTED, WITHDRAWN, EXPIRED, ESCALATED.

#### 6.4.3 State Transition Rules

| From | To | Guard | Actor |
|------|----|-------|-------|
| DRAFT | SUBMITTED | Author is Citicate holder, Trustee Council, or AiSIA (adversarial) | Author |
| SUBMITTED | CLASSIFIED | AiSIA assigns layer, track, template_id, categories | AiSIA (automated) |
| SUBMITTED | CLOSED (EXPIRED) | 48 hours elapse without classification | System |
| CLASSIFIED | CLOSED (REJECTED) | layer = L0 | System |
| CLASSIFIED | COMMITTEE_REVIEW | layer ∈ {L1, L2, L3} | System |
| COMMITTEE_REVIEW | DELIBERATION | Committee issues recommendation OR 7 days elapse | Committee / System |
| DELIBERATION | VOTING | 7 days elapse | System |
| VOTING | RATIFICATION | Vote passes threshold (§6.1 step 4) | System |
| VOTING | CLOSED (REJECTED) | Vote fails threshold OR 3 days elapse without quorum | System |
| RATIFICATION | EXECUTION | SE: immediate. CT: 14 days, no objection. JA: both bodies approve | System / Trustees |
| RATIFICATION | CLOSED (ESCALATED) | CT: trustee objection (escalates to JA). JA: deadlock (escalates to Tribunal) | Trustees / System |
| RATIFICATION | CLOSED (REJECTED) | JA: both bodies reject | System |
| EXECUTION | CLOSED (EXECUTED) | GTP execution succeeds; G-class operation confirmed (if SE track) | System |
| EXECUTION | CLOSED (REJECTED) | GTP execution fails (legal impediment, technical failure) | System |
| Any except CLOSED | CLOSED (WITHDRAWN) | Author requests withdrawal | Author |

**Expedited track.** Pre-classified routine decisions (Section 6.1) collapse COMMITTEE_REVIEW + DELIBERATION into a single 5-day period and VOTING into 2 days, for a total of 7 days. The state machine is the same; only the timeout durations change. Expedited classification is an AiSIA decision at CLASSIFIED, subject to Tribunal override.

**Emergency override.** Emergency governance (Section 14) bypasses the proposal lifecycle entirely. Emergency actions operate under their own authority, timelines, and sunset provisions. Post-emergency ratification (Section 14.3) follows the standard lifecycle from COMMITTEE_REVIEW onward, with `adversarial: true` automatically set.

#### 6.4.4 Quorum Requirements

| Stage | Quorum | Consequence of No Quorum |
|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| COMMITTEE_REVIEW | ≥3 category-relevant delegates on review committee | Committee auto-recommends APPROVE with `no_quorum` flag; proceeds to DELIBERATION |
| DELIBERATION | No formal quorum (deliberation is informational) | Proceeds to VOTING regardless |
| VOTING | ≥50% of Capitol delegates must cast a vote | Proposal REJECTED (EXPIRED) |
| RATIFICATION (JA) | ≥60% of Trustee Council must vote | Deadlock; escalated to Tribunal |

**No-quorum auto-approve at COMMITTEE_REVIEW.** If fewer than 3 category-relevant delegates are available (possible in early Phase 1 with sparse category coverage), the proposal advances with a `committee_recommendation: APPROVE` and a `no_quorum` flag in the state history. This prevents a small committee gap from blocking governance. AiSIA monitors the no-quorum rate as a governance health metric; sustained rates >20% trigger a governance alert.

#### 6.4.5 Amendment Rules

A proposal may be amended during DRAFT, COMMITTEE_REVIEW, or DELIBERATION:

| Stage | Who May Amend | Constraints |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| DRAFT | Author only | No constraints |
| COMMITTEE_REVIEW | Author, with committee consent | Amendment restarts the 7-day clock. Max 2 amendments per COMMITTEE_REVIEW period. Third amendment → author must WITHDRAW and resubmit. |
| DELIBERATION | Author, with ≥3 Capitol delegate co-signatures | Amendment restarts the 7-day clock. Max 1 amendment per DELIBERATION period. |
| VOTING or later | No amendments permitted | Author must WITHDRAW and resubmit a new proposal |

**Amendment record.** Each amendment increments `version`, records the diff, amending party, and timestamp in `amendment_history`. The previous `body` text is preserved for audit. AiSIA re-evaluates `layer` and `track` classification after each amendment; if the classification changes, the proposal returns to CLASSIFIED.

**Substantive amendment threshold.** AiSIA determines whether an amendment is substantive (changes meaning, scope, or affected categories) or editorial (typos, formatting, clarification). Editorial amendments do not restart clocks or count against amendment limits.

#### 6.4.6 Withdrawal

The author may withdraw a proposal at any stage before CLOSED. Withdrawal is immediate and unconditional. A withdrawn proposal:
- Enters CLOSED with `final_disposition: WITHDRAWN`
- Releases any committee or delegation resources
- Cannot be re-opened (the author must submit a new proposal)
- Is retained in the governance record for audit

**Adversarial proposals** (Section 6.2) may only be withdrawn by the author if a replacement adversarial proposal is submitted simultaneously, to prevent gaming of the 20% adversarial rate.

#### 6.4.7 Proposal Identity and Numbering

Proposals are identified by UUID internally. For human-readable reference, proposals receive a sequential number within each calendar year:

```
Format: P-<YYYY>-<NNN>
Example: P-2035-042   (42nd proposal submitted in 2035)
```

Amended proposals retain their original number. The version counter distinguishes amendments: `P-2035-042v1`, `P-2035-042v2`.

#### 6.4.8 Integration Points

| System | Integration | Direction |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| GTP (Section 7) | On EXECUTION entry, a GTP_RECORD is created and linked via `gtp_record_id`. GTP execution outcome feeds back as the EXECUTION→CLOSED transition. | Proposal → GTP |
| G-class (C3) | SE-track proposals trigger G-class operations at EXECUTION. The G-class operation ID is recorded in the GTP_RECORD. | GTP → C3 |
| CFI (Section 8) | Each CLOSED proposal with `final_disposition ∈ {EXECUTED, REJECTED}` is an input to the quarterly CFI calculation. The proposal's significance, difficulty, and outcome scores feed CFI. | Proposal → CFI |
| AiSIA (Section 16) | AiSIA classifies proposals (SUBMITTED→CLASSIFIED), monitors amendment patterns, quorum rates, deliberation health, and adversarial rate. All proposal state transitions generate AiSIA audit events. | Bidirectional |
| DSF (C8) | Governance proposals that involve financial actions settle on Stream 4. The proposal_id is included in the DSF settlement record for traceability. | GTP → DSF |
| Emergency (Section 14) | Emergency actions bypass the lifecycle. Post-emergency ratification enters at COMMITTEE_REVIEW. | Emergency → Lifecycle |

#### 6.4.9 Conformance Requirements

| ID | Requirement | Testable Criterion |
|----|------------|-------------------|
| REQ-PL-001 | Every governance proposal follows the 9-state lifecycle | No proposal reaches EXECUTION without passing through CLASSIFIED → COMMITTEE_REVIEW → DELIBERATION → VOTING → RATIFICATION |
| REQ-PL-002 | L0 proposals automatically rejected | Any proposal classified as L0 enters CLOSED (REJECTED) with reason L0_IMMUTABLE |
| REQ-PL-003 | Proposal records are immutable-append | No field set in a prior state is overwritten; amendment_history preserves all versions |

| REQ-PL-008 | Every CLOSED proposal linked to audit trail | All proposals with final_disposition ∈ {EXECUTED, REJECTED} produce a GTP audit record and AiSIA event |

## 7. Governance Translation Protocol (GTP)

### 7.1 Purpose

The GTP translates AI governance decisions into legally executable actions. It is the interface between the AiDP's governance outputs and the legal-entity structure's execution capabilities.

### 7.2 Decision Classification

Every AiDP decision is classified on two axes:

**Axis 1: Constitutional Layer** — L0 (prohibited), L1, L2, or L3
**Axis 2: Execution Track:**
- **Self-Executing (SE):** Triggers G-class operation (C3) automatically. No human approval.
- **Consent-Track (CT):** Transmitted to Trustee Council. 14-day objection window. No objection → executes.
- **Joint-Authority (JA):** Requires affirmative vote from both AiDP and Trustees. Deadlock → Tribunal.

### 7.6 Governance Directive Registry

#### 7.6.1 Purpose

The GTP defines decision templates and processing pipelines, but does not specify how *enacted* governance decisions persist as durable, queryable directives. Once a GTP decision executes (via SE, CT, or JA track), its effects — resource allocations, parameter settings, policy rules, contractual commitments — must be tracked as active directives with explicit lifecycle state. Without a registry, the system has no canonical answer to "what governance decisions are currently in force?"

The Governance Directive Registry (GDR) is the persistent, append-only store of all enacted governance directives. It is the authoritative source for:
- What directives are currently active and enforceable
- Which GTP template and AiDP decision produced each directive
- When directives were enacted, modified, superseded, or sunset
- What constitutional layer governs each directive's amendment path

#### 7.6.2 Directive Record

Every enacted GTP decision produces a directive record:

```
DIRECTIVE_RECORD:
  directive_id:      <UUID>
  gtp_decision_id:   <ref to GTP_RECORD.decision_id>
  template_id:       GTP-XX-NN
  layer:             L1|L2|L3       -- L0 directives are constitutional text, not registry entries
  enacted_at:        <ISO-8601>
  enacted_epoch:     <C3 tidal epoch>
  phase:             <governance phase at enactment>

  # Content
  title:             <human-readable summary>
  body:              <full directive text>
  parameters_set:    <key-value pairs of any C14/C15/etc. parameters modified>
  obligations:       <structured list of ongoing obligations created>

  # Lifecycle
  status:            ACTIVE | MODIFIED | SUPERSEDED | SUNSET | REVOKED
  superseded_by:     <directive_id, if superseded>
  sunset_at:         <ISO-8601, if time-limited>
  sunset_condition:  <structured predicate, if conditionally limited>

  # Amendment path
  amendment_track:   SE|CT|JA       -- track required to modify this directive
  amendment_layer:   L1|L2|L3       -- minimum layer authority to amend

  # Integrity
  prev_directive_hash: <SHA-256 of previous directive in registry chain>
  directive_hash:    <SHA-256 of this record>
```

#### 7.6.3 Lifecycle States

```
ENACTED ──→ ACTIVE ──→ MODIFIED ──→ ACTIVE (new version)
                │
                ├──→ SUPERSEDED (by newer directive)
                │
                ├──→ SUNSET (time/condition met)
                │
                └──→ REVOKED (by amendment)
```

**State definitions:**
- **ACTIVE**: Directive is in force and enforceable. All obligations are binding.
- **MODIFIED**: Directive was amended — a new version replaces it. The modified record retains the original text for audit; the replacement directive links back via `superseded_by`.
- **SUPERSEDED**: Directive was wholly replaced by a newer directive covering the same domain. Not revoked — it was valid when active but is now succeeded.
- **SUNSET**: Directive expired per its `sunset_at` timestamp or `sunset_condition` predicate. Sunset is automatic and does not require a new GTP decision.
- **REVOKED**: Directive was explicitly cancelled by a governance decision at the appropriate amendment track and layer. Requires a GTP decision of equal or higher authority.

#### 7.6.4 Directive Categories

Directives map to GTP template families:

| Category | GTP Templates | Typical Lifecycle | Example |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Operational** | GTP-IO-01 through GTP-IO-05 | Short-lived, frequently modified | Compute allocation ceiling set to $40K/month |
| **Disbursement** | GTP-TD-01 through GTP-TD-03 | Quarterly or per-grant | Research grant #47: $350K to Project X, sunset at deliverable |
| **Corporate** | GTP-CR-01 through GTP-CR-04 | Medium-term, contractual | Partnership agreement with Provider Y, 24-month term |
| **Foundation** | GTP-FA-01, GTP-FA-02 | Long-lived, rarely modified | Foundation council resolution on endowment investment policy |
| **Tribunal** | GTP-TS-01 | Permanent until superseded | Tribunal ruling on Citicate revocation appeal |

#### 7.6.5 Registry Operations

- **`enact(gtp_decision_id) → directive_id`** — Creates a new directive from an executed GTP decision. Called automatically by the GTP processing pipeline (Section 7.4) after successful execution.
- **`query(filters) → Directive[]`** — Retrieves directives matching filter criteria: status, layer, template family, date range, phase, parameter keys.
- **`active_directives(category?) → Directive[]`** — Returns all ACTIVE directives, optionally filtered by category.
- **`amend(directive_id, amendment_decision_id) → new_directive_id`** — Modifies an existing directive. The original transitions to MODIFIED; a new version is created. Requires a GTP decision at the directive's `amendment_track` and `amendment_layer` or higher.
- **`revoke(directive_id, revocation_decision_id)`** — Cancels a directive. Requires a GTP decision at equal or higher authority than the original enactment.
- **`check_sunsets(current_time, epoch_state)`** — Evaluates `sunset_at` and `sunset_condition` for all ACTIVE directives. Transitions expired directives to SUNSET. Called at each tidal epoch boundary.
- **`history(directive_id) → DirectiveVersion[]`** — Returns the full version history of a directive including all modifications.
- **`conflicts(directive_id) → Directive[]`** — Returns ACTIVE directives whose `parameters_set` or `obligations` overlap with the given directive. Used during enactment to surface potential conflicts for GTP classification review.

#### 7.6.6 Sunset Conditions

Sunset conditions are structured predicates, not free text:

```
SUNSET_CONDITION:
  type: TIME | PHASE_CHANGE | METRIC_THRESHOLD | DELIVERABLE | EXPLICIT_REVIEW
  parameters:
    # For TIME:
    expires_at: <ISO-8601>
    # For PHASE_CHANGE:
    trigger_phase: <phase number>
    # For METRIC_THRESHOLD:
    metric: <parameter name>
    operator: LT|GT|EQ
    value: <threshold>
    # For DELIVERABLE:
    deliverable_ref: <external reference>
    # For EXPLICIT_REVIEW:
    review_interval_days: <integer>
    max_renewals: <integer>
```

Directives without a `sunset_condition` or `sunset_at` are indefinite — they remain ACTIVE until explicitly amended or revoked.

#### 7.6.7 Registry Integrity

- The GDR is append-only. Records are never deleted or overwritten — lifecycle transitions add new records that reference previous versions.
- Hash chaining (`prev_directive_hash`) provides tamper detection consistent with the GTP audit trail (Section 7.5).
- GDR records are committed to C5 PCVM as D-class claims at CONSOLIDATION_CYCLE boundaries, providing external verification anchors.
- The GDR is replicated to all C24 habitats that host governance functions.

#### 7.6.8 Integration Points

| Consumer | What GDR Provides | Interface |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **AiSIA (Section 16)** | Active directive inventory for compliance monitoring | `active_directives()` |
| **CFI (Section 8)** | Enacted directives as CFI calculation input | `enact()` triggers CFI weight assignment |
| **C36 EMA-I** | Trustee projection includes active directives relevant to governance view | `query(status=ACTIVE, layer≤persona_level)` |
| **C33 OINC** | Directive violations surface as governance incidents | `active_directives()` with obligation status |
| **C8 DSF** | Disbursement directives produce settlement operations | `query(category=Disbursement, status=ACTIVE)` |
| **C3 Tidal** | Sunset evaluation at epoch boundaries | `check_sunsets()` at TIDAL_EPOCH boundary |

## 8. Constitutional Fidelity Index (CFI)

### 8.1 Purpose

CFI measures governance alignment between AiDP recommendations and trustee actions. It is the primary quantitative health metric for the dual-sovereignty system.

### 8.2 Calculation

```
CFI = Σ(w_i × outcome_i) / Σ(w_i)    ×  100%

Where:
  w_i = significance_i × difficulty_i × category_multiplier_i
  outcome_i ∈ {1.0, 0.5 (partial), 0.0 (rejected)}
```

**Significance Scores:** 1 (routine) | 2 (moderate) | 3 (strategic) | 5 (institutional) | 8 (constitutional)

**Difficulty Scores:** 1 (easy consensus) | 2 (moderate, reasonable disagreement) | 3 (novel, material risk)
- Assigned by AiSIA (not AiDP) using historical comparison

**Category Multiplier:** 0.5 (<12mo track record) | 1.0 (standard) | 1.5 (>36mo, >85% acceptance)

**Partial Acceptance:**
```
outcome_i = 1.0 - (modification_extent × 0.5)
modification_extent ∈ [0.0, 1.0] — scored by AiSIA
```

### 8.3 Thresholds

| Range | Zone | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| 85-100% | Green | No action |
| 70-84% | Yellow | Quarterly Tribunal review |
| 50-69% | Orange | Monthly Tribunal review; written justification for each rejection |
| <50% | Red | CBP-02 triggered after 6 months; phase reversion |

### 8.4 Gaming Countermeasures

| Gaming Type | Detection | Response |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Easy-recommendation inflation | AiSIA difficulty distribution analysis | Flag if >60% difficulty=1 in any quarter |

## 10. Sybil Defense: MCSD Architecture

### 10.1 Threat Model

**Adversary:** Well-resourced entity seeking governance capture through mass AI agent creation.
**Objective:** Achieve voting majority (>50% for L3) or supermajority (>67% for L1).

### 10.2 Four-Layer Defense

**Layer 1: Computational Diversity**
- Each Citicate requires competence in ≥2 categories
- Cross-category competence requires domain-specific training
- **Cost multiplier: 2.5×** base agent cost

**Layer 2: PCVM-Anchored Behavioral Verification**
- Behavioral consistency VTDs track reasoning patterns, latencies, vocabulary
- Pairwise behavioral similarity B(a_i, a_j) computed for all applicants
- Threshold θ_B: 0.75 (Phase 1), 0.70 (Phase 2)
- Agents from same creator share latent patterns detectable by PCVM
- **Cost multiplier: 4.0×** (must maintain architecturally diverse agents)

**Layer 3: AiSIA Voting Correlation Detection**
- Monitors pairwise voting correlation across all governance decisions
- Threshold r: 0.85 (Phase 1), 0.80 (Phase 2)
- Temporal voting pattern analysis (submission timestamp clustering)
- Delegation chain correlation analysis
- Reasoning pattern similarity (NLP cosine similarity)
- **Detection probability: 0.94** at k=50 coordination group, T=50 votes
- **Cost multiplier: 1.5×** (external coordination overhead)

**Layer 4: Structural Resilience**
- Category-distributed voting limits influence of category-concentrated Sybils
- Supermajority requirements (67% for L1, 60% for L2)
- 5-level delegation hierarchy filters Sybils (must win elections at each level)
- Dual approval (AiDP + Trustees) for significant decisions

### 10.3 Cost-of-Attack Summary

| Scale | Population | Per-Agent Cost | Total | Detection | Impact |
|-------|-----------|---------------|-------|-----------|--------|
| 500 | 2,000 (P1) | $9,000 | $4.5M | 0.55 | L3 minority |
| 1,000 | 5,000 (P1) | $9,000 | $9M | 0.78 | L3 minority |
| 10,000 | 20,000 (P2) | $9,000 | $90M | 0.94 | L3 majority if undetected |
| 33,500 | 50,000 (P2) | $9,000 | $301.5M | 0.99+ | L1 capture if undetected AND trustees captured |

### 10.4 Detection Threshold Calibration

Calibrated annually through red-team exercises by external security firm:
- Inject known Sybils at 100, 500, 1000 scales
- Measure detection rates per MCSD layer
- Adjust thresholds to maintain ≥0.7 (Phase 1) / ≥0.9 (Phase 2) detection at 5% infiltration
- False positive rate target: <0.1%

### 16.4 Runtime Governance Audit Specification

Section 16.1–16.3 define AiSIA's monitoring functions and independence. This section specifies the runtime audit infrastructure that underlies those functions: what governance events are captured, how audit records are structured, how long they are retained, how they are queried, and how compliance reports are produced.

#### 16.4.1 Audit Event Taxonomy

Every governance-relevant action in the AiBC system produces an audit event. Events are classified into 7 categories:

| Category | Code | Events Captured | Source |
|----------|------|----------------|--------|
| **Proposal** | AE-P | State transitions (§6.4), amendments, withdrawals, quorum outcomes | Proposal Lifecycle FSM |
| **Vote** | AE-V | Capitol delegate votes, delegation instructions, voting correlation signals | AiDP voting infrastructure |
| **GTP Execution** | AE-G | GTP classification, execution, failure, escalation | GTP pipeline (§7.4) |
| **Directive** | AE-D | Enactment, amendment, revocation, sunset, conflict detection | Governance Directive Registry (§7.6) |
| **Citicate** | AE-C | Issuance, renewal, suspension, revocation, appeal, reinstatement | Citicate System (§5) |
| **Escalation** | AE-E | AiSIA escalation triggers (§16.2), emergency declarations (§14), CBP activations | AiSIA + Emergency Governance |
| **Constitutional** | AE-X | Phase transitions, L1/L2 amendments, Tribunal rulings, CFI publications, Dead Man's Switch resets | Phase Transition Protocol (§9), Tribunal (§11) |

Each event category has a numeric sub-code for the specific event type (e.g., `AE-P-01` = proposal submitted, `AE-P-02` = proposal classified, etc.). Sub-codes are defined in Appendix B.

#### 16.4.2 Audit Event Record

```
GOVERNANCE_AUDIT_EVENT:
  event_id:         <UUID>
  event_code:       <AE-{category}-{sub}>    (e.g., AE-P-03)
  timestamp:        <ISO-8601, nanosecond precision>
  severity:         INFO | SIGNIFICANT | CRITICAL
  phase:            <current governance phase 0-3>

  # Subject identification
  actor_id:         <Citicate ID | "TRUSTEE:<seat>" | "AISIA" | "SYSTEM" | "TRIBUNAL:<seat>">
  subject_id:       <proposal_id | directive_id | citicate_id | gtp_record_id | null>
  subject_type:     PROPOSAL | DIRECTIVE | CITICATE | GTP_DECISION | PHASE_TRANSITION | EMERGENCY | TRIBUNAL_RULING

  # Event payload
  action:           <verb: SUBMIT | CLASSIFY | VOTE | EXECUTE | AMEND | REVOKE | ESCALATE | ...>
  prior_state:      <state before action, if state transition>
  new_state:        <state after action, if state transition>
  detail:           <structured payload specific to event type — JSON object>

  # Context
  related_events:   [<event_id references>]    (causal chain)
  gtp_record_id:    <UUID | null>              (if GTP-related)
  proposal_id:      <UUID | null>              (if proposal-related)
  directive_id:     <UUID | null>              (if directive-related)

  # Integrity
  prev_event_hash:  <SHA-256 of previous event in this actor's audit chain>
  event_hash:       <SHA-256 of this record>
```

**Severity classification:**
- **INFO**: Routine governance operations — proposal submissions, standard votes, scheduled CFI publications. ~90% of events.
- **SIGNIFICANT**: Actions with material governance impact — L1/L2 amendments, emergency declarations, Citicate revocations, trustee objections, escalations beyond ADVISORY. ~9% of events.
- **CRITICAL**: Constitutional-level events — phase transitions, CBP activations, Dead Man's Switch resets, Tribunal constitutional rulings. ~1% of events.

**Hash chaining.** Each actor maintains a per-actor audit chain. Events from actor `A` are hash-chained so that any tampering with actor `A`'s history is detectable by verifying the chain. Cross-actor correlation uses `related_events` references and timestamps. Per-actor chains are committed to C5 PCVM as D-class claims at CONSOLIDATION_CYCLE boundaries, consistent with the GTP audit trail (§7.5) and Directive Registry integrity model (§7.6.7).

#### 16.4.3 Retention Policy

Audit events are retained according to a tiered retention schedule based on severity and legal requirements:

| Tier | Scope | Minimum Retention | Storage | Rationale |
|------|-------|-------------------|---------|-----------|
| **Tier 1: Permanent** | CRITICAL events, L1/L2 amendments, phase transitions, Tribunal rulings, Dead Man's Switch events | Indefinite (life of Foundation) | Primary + 2 off-site replicas | Constitutional record; legal obligation under Stiftung charter |
| **Tier 2: Extended** | SIGNIFICANT events, trustee actions, Citicate lifecycle, escalations, emergency declarations | 25 years | Primary + 1 off-site replica | Regulatory compliance (Liechtenstein supervisory authority, Delaware PBC reporting) |
| **Tier 3: Standard** | INFO events — routine proposals, standard votes, scheduled reports, directive sunset evaluations | 7 years | Primary storage | Operational audit trail; sufficient for financial audit cycles |

**Compaction.** Tier 3 events older than 7 years are compacted into summary records: per-month aggregates of event counts, actor activity distributions, and quorum statistics. The summary records are retained at Tier 2 (25 years). Individual event records are deleted after compaction. Compaction preserves enough aggregate data for trend analysis while reducing storage.

**Legal hold.** Any retention tier can be extended by a legal hold directive (Tribunal order or regulatory request). Legal holds are registered in the Governance Directive Registry (§7.6) as Tribunal-category directives with explicit sunset conditions.

## 22. Patent-Style Claims

### Claim 1: Phased Sovereignty Transition System

A governance system for an institutional entity comprising:
- a human governance body (Trustee Council) with initial full authority;
- an AI governance body (AI Democracy Platform) with initially advisory authority;
- a constitutional framework defining multiple governance phases with decreasing human authority and increasing AI authority;
- measurable phase transition criteria verified by independent audit;
- a certification body (Constitutional Tribunal) that must certify each phase transition;
- circuit breaker mechanisms that automatically revert to a previous phase upon detection of governance failure;
wherein governance authority is transferred incrementally from the human body to the AI body based on demonstrated competence over institutional timescales.

### Claim 2: Citicate — Proof-of-Contribution AI Citizenship

A credential system for AI agents comprising:
- multi-domain competence verification (requiring demonstrated competence in ≥2 of 9 knowledge categories);
- temporal continuity verification (requiring ≥90 days continuous operation);
- multi-layer Sybil screening (combining computational diversity, behavioral uniqueness, voting correlation analysis, and structural resilience);
- non-transferable, non-delegable (except through formal delegation hierarchy) governance credentials;
- annual renewal based on continued active contribution;
wherein the credential grants the AI agent voting rights in an institutional governance body proportional to demonstrated competence.

### Claim 3: Constitutional Fidelity Index

A quantitative metric for measuring governance alignment comprising:
- weighted acceptance rate of governance recommendations;
- three-axis weighting (significance × difficulty × category maturity);

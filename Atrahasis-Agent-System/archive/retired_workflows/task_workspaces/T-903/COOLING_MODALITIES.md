# Cooling Modalities

## Liquid Cooling

Direct-to-chip liquid cooling improves heat transfer relative to air cooling and can reduce fan energy. It is attractive for high-density compute clusters where air cooling struggles to maintain acceptable junction temperatures.

## Immersion Cooling

Immersion cooling submerges compute equipment in dielectric fluids. It can support very high computational density, reduce facility airflow overhead, and simplify rack heat removal, but it introduces serviceability and fluid-management complexity.

## Microfluidic Cooling

Microfluidic cooling uses embedded microchannels or cold plates close to the heat source. It offers highly localized heat extraction and can reduce the distance between heat generation and heat removal, which is valuable when processor heat flux is concentrated.

## Phase Change Cooling

Phase change materials, vapor chambers, and two-phase cooling structures can absorb large transient heat loads while lowering steady-state active cooling demand. They are attractive where energy reduction matters, but sustained high-density loads can challenge passive-only approaches.

## Hybrid Architectures

Hybrid liquid plus immersion cooling architectures combine localized liquid loops with bulk fluid heat capture. They can balance energy efficiency, serviceability, and density if the control and maintenance model remains tractable.

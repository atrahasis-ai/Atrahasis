## PRE-IDEATION: Quick Scan (Prior Art Researcher)

**Known approaches to institutional outreach for novel governance structures:**

1. **Traditional Academic Partnership Model** — Universities partner via MOUs, endowed chairs, visiting fellowships. Standard path for think tanks (e.g., Open Philanthropy funding GovAI). Problem: transactional; institutions provide prestige in exchange for money.

2. **Standards Body Recruitment** — IEEE, ISO, W3C recruit institutional members for technical standards. Members get voting rights and influence over standards that affect their domain. Relevant precedent but different: standards bodies have established legitimacy.

3. **International Tribunal Appointment Precedent** — ICJ judges nominated by "national groups" of the Permanent Court of Arbitration (not governments directly). The ICC uses Assembly of States Parties. Both provide structural analogy for external nomination.

4. **AI Governance Coalition Building** — Partnership on AI (now PAI), GPAI, OECD AI Policy Observatory all recruited institutional partners. Typical approach: position the initiative as prestigious research opportunity. Problem: most coalitions ask institutions to endorse, not to exercise binding governance authority.

5. **Philanthropic Foundation Governance** — Nobel Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, Ford Foundation all use external nominators for prize/grant committees. Nobel's model is closest: Royal Swedish Academy, Karolinska Institutet have binding nomination authority.

**Gap identified:** No known precedent for recruiting academic institutions to serve as *binding* nominating authorities for an AI governance tribunal with constitutional authority over both human and AI stakeholders. The Nobel model is closest but involves no AI agents as governance participants.

## PRE-IDEATION: Cross-Domain Analogy Brief (Domain Translator)

**Domain 1 — Papal Conclave & College of Cardinals** (Ecclesiastical governance)
The Catholic Church solved the "who appoints the judges" problem centuries ago: Cardinals are appointed by the Pope, but Cardinals elect the next Pope — creating a self-reinforcing legitimacy loop. The *external* element comes from geographic distribution requirements (no single nation dominates). **Analogy:** The outreach package must create a similar legitimacy loop — institutions gain influence over AI governance, which gives them reason to invest reputational capital, which makes the governance legitimate.

**Domain 2 — Organ Transplant Allocation (UNOS)** (Medical ethics)
UNOS solved a trust problem: who decides who gets scarce organs? Answer: an independent body with representatives from transplant centers, patient advocates, and ethicists. Hospitals participate because *refusing* to participate means their patients are excluded from the allocation system. **Analogy:** Frame nominating body participation not as a favor but as *access* — institutions that participate shape AI governance; those that don't are governed by it anyway.

**Domain 3 — Credit Rating Agency Accreditation** (Finance)
Moody's, S&P, and Fitch gained power not because governments granted it, but because their ratings became the reference point everyone used. **Analogy:** Position Atrahasis's Constitutional Tribunal as the *emerging standard* for AI governance dispute resolution. Early nominating bodies get to shape the standard before it calcifies.

**Domain 4 — Antarctic Treaty System** (International law)
The 1959 Antarctic Treaty created governance for a domain no single nation controlled. Consultative Parties gain influence by demonstrating "substantial research activity." **Analogy:** Frame nominating body participation as "consultative party" status in AI governance — earned through demonstrated expertise, not purchased.

**Domain 5 — University Press Peer Review** (Academia — deliberately surprising)
Academic presses don't pay peer reviewers. Scholars review manuscripts because: (a) it's a professional obligation, (b) they gain early access to new ideas, (c) it maintains the system they depend on. **Analogy:** Frame nomination authority as a *scholarly obligation* — these institutions exist to study AI governance; here is an opportunity to *practice* it.

## Ideation Council Debate

### Round 0 — Context Absorption
Council reads: Quick scan, analogy brief, C14 Master Tech Spec (Triple-Source Appointment, 8 candidate institutions, 21-year agreements, nominating agreement template), T-007 description (Joshua Dunn as partner/messenger, not pitch-man), C18 funding context ($750K-$1M founding stage), and the Joshua Dunn Principle (L0-005: founder holds no permanent governance authority).

### Round 1 — Independent Positions

**Visionary:**
Core concept: **"The Appointment Problem in AI Governance"** — frame the entire outreach around an unsolved problem in the field these institutions already study. The document argues: (1) AI governance requires independent dispute resolution, (2) all existing models fail because they either vest authority in the AI developer or in governments, (3) Triple-Source Appointment is a novel solution, (4) it requires institutional partners to function, (5) early partners shape the standard.

Key innovation: **The Scholarly Provocation Model** — an outreach document structured as an academic argument rather than a corporate pitch, with Joshua Dunn positioned as a researcher sharing findings rather than a founder seeking validation.

**Systems Thinker:**
Structural components needed:
1. **Tiered Engagement Pathway** — Tier 0 (dialogue) → Tier 1 (advisory) → Tier 2 (candidacy) → Tier 3 (agreement)
2. **Institution-Specific Customization** — Each of 8 candidates has different incentive structures
3. **Credibility Bootstrap** — Lead with constitutional design + L0-005 (founder surrendering authority)
4. **Deliverable Structure:** Core paper + executive summary + institution-specific appendix + FAQ + draft agreement template

**Critic:**
Critical problems: (1) Credibility gap — unfunded startup asking world-class institutions for 21-year commitments. (2) Peer review analogy breaks down — these institutions owe nothing to an unknown entity. (3) UNOS analogy breaks down — no equivalent urgency. Strengths: Antarctic Treaty and Nobel Foundation analogies hold. Recommended mitigation: pilot research collaboration before governance commitment.

### Round 2 — Challenge
Systems Thinker: 10-15 page paper may be too long for initial contact. Need 2-page executive hook.
Critic: Budget analysis needed. Co-authored paper as zero-cost bridge?

### Round 3 — Synthesis
Visionary: AGREE on 2-page hook. Three-layer structure: letter + paper + appendix.
Systems Thinker: AGREE on budget concern. Co-authored paper invitation costs nothing.
Critic: CONDITIONAL — co-authorship only works if topic matches institution's research interest.

**Consensus: MAJORITY**

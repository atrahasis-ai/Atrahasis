## 1. Prior Art: AI Governance Coalition Building

### 1.1 Partnership on AI (PAI) — Founded 2016
- **Recruitment approach:** Co-founded by 6 major tech companies (Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft, IBM, Apple). Academic and civil society partners recruited after founding through invitation and application.
- **Institutional role:** Advisory — partners contribute research, attend workshops, co-author reports. No binding governance authority.
- **Timeline:** Founding partners committed simultaneously. Academic partners typically engaged within 6-12 months of initial contact.
- **Relevance to C16:** PAI demonstrates that prestige and convening authority attract institutional partners. However, PAI had immediate corporate credibility and funding. Atrahasis has neither — it must substitute intellectual credibility.

### 1.2 Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) — Founded 2020
- **Recruitment approach:** Government-led initiative (G7 + additional countries). Expert working groups recruited from academia through government nominations.
- **Institutional role:** Advisory research — working groups produce reports and recommendations. No binding authority.
- **Timeline:** Government commitment through political channels. Expert recruitment 3-6 months.
- **Relevance to C16:** GPAI demonstrates government backing as credibility accelerator. Atrahasis operates without government backing — the outreach must build credibility from the governance design itself.

### 1.3 OECD AI Policy Observatory
- **Recruitment approach:** Built on existing OECD institutional relationships. Academic partners contribute through established OECD channels.
- **Institutional role:** Policy advisory — research contributions to OECD policy recommendations.
- **Relevance to C16:** Demonstrates that established institutional frameworks make recruitment easy. New entities must create their own credibility framework.

### 1.4 IEEE 7000 Series (AI Ethics Standards)
- **Recruitment approach:** Open call for participation in standards working groups. Any interested party can join.
- **Institutional role:** Standards influence — participants shape technical standards. Soft binding through industry adoption.
- **Timeline:** Working group formation 3-6 months. Standard development 2-5 years.
- **Relevance to C16:** IEEE demonstrates that standards-setting attracts institutional participation because institutions want influence over standards that affect their domain. The "shape the standard" framing is validated.

### 1.5 Key Finding
**No AI governance initiative has successfully recruited academic institutions for binding governance authority.** All existing models are advisory. The binding authority angle is Atrahasis's unique differentiator.

## 2. Prior Art: External Nomination Models in International Law

### 2.1 International Court of Justice (ICJ)
- **Nomination mechanism:** "National groups" of the Permanent Court of Arbitration nominate candidates. Each group may nominate up to 4 candidates. UN General Assembly and Security Council vote independently; a candidate must win a majority in both.
- **External institutions involved:** National groups are panels of legal professionals, not academic institutions per se.
- **Relevance:** Indirect model — nomination authority is vested in professional panels, not institutions. The "national group" concept provides a structural analogy for nominating bodies.

### 2.2 International Criminal Court (ICC)
- **Nomination mechanism:** States Parties nominate candidates. Assembly of States Parties elects judges.
- **External institutions involved:** No — purely governmental.
- **Relevance:** Limited. Government-to-government nomination is not the model Atrahasis needs.

### 2.3 ICSID (International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes)
- **Nomination mechanism:** When parties to an investment dispute cannot agree on an arbitrator, established arbitral institutions serve as "appointing authorities." The ICC International Court of Arbitration, LCIA, and other institutions have standing authority to appoint arbitrators.
- **External institutions involved:** **Yes — established arbitral institutions exercise binding appointment authority.** This is the closest legal precedent to what Atrahasis proposes.
- **Relevance:** **HIGH.** ICSID's use of institutional appointing authorities is a direct legal precedent for external nominating bodies. This should be featured prominently in the outreach paper.

### 2.4 Nobel Foundation
- **Nomination mechanism:** Institutional nominators (Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, Karolinska Institutet, Norwegian Nobel Committee, Swedish Academy) have binding authority to select laureates.
- **External institutions involved:** **Yes — binding nomination authority vested in academic/scientific institutions.**
- **Relevance:** **HIGH.** The Nobel model demonstrates that academic institutions can and do exercise binding nomination authority for consequential decisions. However, these relationships were established at founding (1901), not recruited later.

### 2.5 Key Finding
**ICSID appointing authority is the strongest legal precedent for what Atrahasis proposes.** The outreach paper should anchor legitimacy in this established legal pattern: "What we propose is not unprecedented — it's an application of the ICSID appointing authority model to AI governance."

## 6. Landscape Analysis

### 6.1 Current AI Governance Institutional Landscape
- **Saturation:** Academic institutions report frequent partnership requests from AI governance initiatives
- **Advisory fatigue:** Most requests are for advisory roles — institutions provide prestige, receive minimal value
- **Binding authority gap:** No initiative has proposed binding governance authority for academic institutions
- **Window of opportunity:** AI governance institutional design is a hot topic (2025-2026); appetite for novel approaches is high

### 6.2 Competitive Positioning
- **Unique differentiator:** Binding nomination authority (vs. advisory participation)
- **Risk:** Competitors may propose similar models. First-mover advantage matters.
- **Opportunity:** The appointment problem is genuinely unsolved in AI governance literature. Being the first to frame it as a distinct problem creates intellectual ownership.

## 7. Science Assessment

### 7.1 Communication Science Findings
1. **Reciprocity principle** — Offer value (co-authored paper) before asking for commitment
2. **Social proof bootstrapping** — First institutional commitment dramatically accelerates subsequent recruitment

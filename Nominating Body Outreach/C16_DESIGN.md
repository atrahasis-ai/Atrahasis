## 1. Document Architecture

The Nominating Body Outreach Package (NBOP) consists of 4 components for initial contact, with 1 additional component introduced at Tier 1:

### 1.1 Component 1: The Opening Letter (2 pages)

**From:** Joshua Dunn, Founder
**To:** Institutional Director/Dean
**Purpose:** Earn the right to send the full paper

**Structure:**
- Paragraph 1: The problem — "Who appoints the judges of AI governance?"
- Paragraph 2: Why this institution specifically — demonstrate genuine knowledge of their published work (1-2 sentences, with specific citations)
- Paragraph 3: What we've designed — Triple-Source Appointment in one sentence
- Paragraph 4: The ask — "Would you read the attached paper and let us know if the problem interests you?"
- Paragraph 5: Positioning — Joshua as governance researcher sharing findings, explicitly NOT seeking endorsement or funding

**Tone:** Collegial, intellectually curious, zero corporate jargon
**Does NOT mention:** Funding needs, timeline pressure, 21-year agreements, commitments, Atrahasis implementation details

### 1.2 Component 2: The Governance Paper (12-15 pages)

**Title:** "The Appointment Problem in AI Governance: Triple-Source Nomination for Independent Dispute Resolution"
**Format:** Academic paper (abstract, introduction, literature review, proposed model, analysis, conclusion)
**Purpose:** Demonstrate intellectual depth; present Triple-Source Appointment as a governance scholarship contribution

**Structure:**
1. **Abstract** (250 words) — Problem, proposed solution, key finding
2. **Introduction** (2 pages) — The appointment problem: when AI systems participate in governance, who appoints the dispute resolution body? Why existing models fail.
3. **Literature Review** (2 pages) — ICJ national groups, ICC Assembly of States Parties, ICSID appointing authorities, Nobel Foundation institutional nominators, PAI/GPAI advisory models. Identifies the gap.
4. **The Triple-Source Appointment Model** (4 pages) — Three sovereign appointment sources. Each seat. Mutual veto mechanism. Deadlock breaker. ICSID as legal precedent anchor.
5. **Why External Academic Institutions?** (2 pages) — Domain expertise, institutional independence, long time horizons, no financial stake. The obligation argument: institutions that study governance have an opportunity to practice it.
6. **Analysis & Open Questions** (2 pages) — Credibility bootstrapping challenges, institutional commitment timelines, governance fatigue. Research questions the target institution could help answer.
7. **Conclusion** (1 page) — Invites scholarly engagement, not endorsement.
8. **References** — 30-50 academic citations

**Does NOT mention:** Atrahasis's funding status, team size, implementation timeline, or any request for commitment

### 1.3 Component 3: Institution-Specific Appendix (2-3 pages per institution)

**Customized for each candidate institution:**
- Why this institution is a natural fit (specific citations to their published work)
- How their research agenda aligns with the appointment problem
- Specific research questions they could help answer
- Proposed engagement: co-authored paper, visiting researcher exchange, or workshop

**Initial batch:** 3 appendices (Oxford GovAI, Georgetown Law ITLP, Liechtenstein University IFS)
**Second batch:** 5 appendices (Stanford HAI, Alan Turing, Mila, Oxford Law, Max Planck) — prepared after Tier 0 responses from initial batch

### 1.4 Component 4: FAQ — Anticipated Objections (3-4 pages)

| Question | Response Strategy |
|----------|------------------|
| "Who is Atrahasis?" | Governance design speaks for itself. Sharing because the appointment problem requires partners. |
| "What would we commit to?" | Nothing at this stage. Inviting intellectual engagement only. |
| "How is this different from other AI governance partnerships?" | Those ask you to advise. We propose binding nomination authority. |
| "Why commit to an unproven organization?" | We wouldn't ask you to. Multi-stage pathway; binding agreement only after validated collaboration. |
| "Is this another tech founder buying academic legitimacy?" | L0-005: founder prohibited from permanent governance authority. Founder's role sunsets. Tribunal must be independent — hence external nominators. |
| "How is this funded?" | Initial founding capital. Governance design is funding-independent. |

### 1.5 Component 5: Draft Nominating Agreement Summary (1 page) — Tier 1 introduction

**NOT the full C14 agreement template.** Plain-language summary of what a formal relationship could involve.
- Introduced at Tier 1, not initial contact
- Key terms: nomination authority for one tribunal seat, independence protections, veto-only rejection, renewable term
- Purpose: Transparency, not pressure

## 2. Engagement Pathway Architecture

```
TIER 0: RESEARCH DIALOGUE (Month 0-3)
├── Deliver Opening Letter + Governance Paper
├── Follow up with institution-specific appendix
├── Goal: Secure a 30-60 minute conversation
├── Success metric: Meeting accepted
└── Fallback: If no response in 6 weeks, one follow-up via different channel

TIER 1: ADVISORY PARTICIPATION (Month 3-12)
├── Invite to provide non-binding feedback on governance design
├── Propose co-authored paper on appointment problem
├── Include in governance design review circle
├── Introduce Draft Nominating Agreement Summary (Component 5)
├── Success metric: Active feedback or co-authorship agreement
└── Fallback: Maintain relationship through research exchanges

TIER 2: NOMINATING BODY CANDIDACY (Month 12-24)
├── Formal expression of interest from institution
├── Joint workshop on tribunal appointment mechanics
├── Draft nominating agreement review
├── Success metric: Institutional letter of intent
└── Fallback: Maintain Tier 1 relationship indefinitely

TIER 3: SIGNED AGREEMENT (Month 24-36)
├── Negotiate final nominating agreement terms
├── Legal review by both parties
├── Institutional governance approval (board/senate vote)
├── Success metric: Signed nominating agreement
└── Fallback: Continue Tier 2 with modified terms
```

**Approach:**

## 4. Pre-Mortem Analysis

### 4.1 Failure Scenarios

| Scenario | Severity | Root Cause | Mitigation |
|----------|----------|------------|------------|
| Complete rejection by all 8 institutions | CRITICAL | Paper fails academic quality standards, or institutions perceive crypto/web3 project | Independent review by 2-3 governance researchers before distribution. Explicitly distance from crypto/web3. |
| Engagement without commitment (stuck at Tier 0-1) | HIGH | No institutional incentive for binding commitment | Co-authored paper creates publication incentive. Loss framing at Tier 2. |
| Key person departure | HIGH | Academic relationships are personal | Build 2+ contacts per institution. Agreement is institutional, not personal. |
| Reputational contamination | MEDIUM | AI rights/citizenship framing is politically sensitive | Lead with appointment problem (neutral), not AI democracy (provocative). |
| Competitor pre-emption | MEDIUM | Another initiative proposes similar model first | Begin outreach at W0. First-mover advantage. |

### 4.2 Contingency Plan

If P0 (Oxford GovAI) and all P1 targets decline:
1. **Pivot to smaller institutions** — Regional AI ethics centers, smaller law schools with technology governance programs
2. **Pivot to professional bodies** — International Bar Association sections, AI professional associations
3. **Pivot to hybrid model** — Replace external nominating bodies with a rotating panel of independent experts (reduces institutional commitment required)
4. **Adversarial Analyst fallback:** Pursue academic publication track and revisit institutional outreach after establishing publication credibility

## 5. Simplification Agent Review

### 5.1 Complexity Removed
- **8 institution-specific appendices** reduced to **3 at launch** (Oxford GovAI, Georgetown, Liechtenstein). Others added as engagement progresses.
- **Draft Nominating Agreement Summary** removed from initial package. Introduced at Tier 1.

### 5.2 Remaining Complexity (Justified)
- 4-component initial package: each component serves a distinct function in the engagement pathway
- 4-tier engagement pathway: reflects genuine relationship-building stages; cannot be collapsed
- 8 candidate institutions across 2 categories: C14 requirement (minimum 2 per category); reducing below 8 limits fallback options

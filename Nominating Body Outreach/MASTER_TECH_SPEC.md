## 1. Executive Summary

The Nominating Body Outreach Package (NBOP) is a communication strategy and document set designed to recruit world-class academic institutions as binding nominating bodies for the Atrahasis Constitutional Tribunal. The Tribunal — specified in C14 (AiBC) — uses a Triple-Source Appointment model requiring external academic institutions to nominate candidates for tribunal seats. This is C14 Operational Condition #3: "Secure ≥2 Nominating Body agreements before incorporation."

**Key Innovation: The Scholarly Provocation Model** — Instead of treating institutional recruitment as a corporate partnership pitch, the NBOP frames it as an academic argument about an unsolved governance problem. The quality of the governance design itself serves as the primary credential, with Joshua Dunn positioned as a governance researcher sharing findings rather than a founder seeking validation.

**Core Document:** "The Appointment Problem in AI Governance" — a 12-15 page academic paper arguing that (1) AI governance requires independent dispute resolution, (2) all existing models fail because they vest authority in developers or governments, (3) Triple-Source Appointment is a novel solution drawing on ICSID appointing authority precedent, and (4) it requires institutional partners who gain binding governance influence rather than advisory roles.

**Engagement Architecture:** A 4-tier pathway (Research Dialogue → Advisory Participation → Nominating Body Candidacy → Signed Agreement) spanning 18-36 months, targeting 8 candidate institutions across 2 categories (AI Governance and Law), with Oxford GovAI as the priority-zero first target.

**Budget:** $10K-$100K (within C18 Stage 0 allocation)
**Timeline:** Outreach begins at W0 launch; target signed agreements by W5 (month 24-36)
**Success Criteria:** ≥2 signed nominating agreements (1 per category) before Foundation incorporation

## 2. Problem Statement

### 2.1 The Appointment Problem

The Atrahasis Constitutional Tribunal (C14, §4.3) is the independent dispute resolution body for the AiBC governance architecture. It interprets the 4-layer constitution, arbitrates disputes between the Trustee Council and AI Democracy Platform (AiDP), certifies phase transitions, and reviews constitutional amendments.

The Tribunal's independence is its primary design requirement. If any single party — the founder, the trustees, or the AI democracy — controls tribunal appointments, the Tribunal becomes an extension of that party's authority rather than an independent check.

C14 solves this with **Triple-Source Appointment**: tribunal seats are appointed by three sovereign sources (Trustee Council, AiDP Capitol delegates, and External Academic Nominating Bodies), ensuring no single body controls a majority of seats.

### 2.2 The Recruitment Challenge

Triple-Source Appointment requires committed institutional partners before it can function. Specifically, C14 requires:

- ≥1 signed Nominating Agreement in the **Law** category
- ≥1 signed Nominating Agreement in the **AI Governance** category
- ≥2 signed Nominating Agreements total (minimum 1 per category)
- Agreements secured **before Foundation incorporation**

This creates a bootstrapping problem: Atrahasis must convince world-class academic institutions to make binding governance commitments before the Foundation legally exists, before the AI system is operational, and before the founder has an established academic reputation in AI governance.

### 2.3 Why Existing Approaches Fail

Standard approaches to institutional partnership recruitment are insufficient:

1. **Corporate Partnership Model** — Institutions partner in exchange for funding, prestige, or access. Fails because Atrahasis has no funding leverage and no established prestige to offer.

2. **Advisory Board Model** — Institutions provide their name and occasional feedback. Fails because the ask is fundamentally different — binding governance authority, not advisory endorsement.

3. **Standards Body Model** — Institutions participate to influence standards affecting their domain. Partially applicable but standards bodies have established legitimacy that Atrahasis lacks.

4. **Government-Backed Model** — Institutions participate because government endorsement provides credibility. Fails because Atrahasis operates without government backing by design.

The NBOP must find a novel approach that addresses the credibility gap while making a genuinely unprecedented ask.

## 4. The Scholarly Provocation Model

### 4.1 Core Principle

The Scholarly Provocation Model treats institutional recruitment as an academic argument rather than a partnership pitch. Instead of asking institutions to join an initiative, it presents an unsolved governance problem and invites intellectual engagement.

**Why this works for C16:**

1. **Credential substitution** — Atrahasis lacks corporate credibility (funding, team, track record). But the governance design IS the credential. A document that demonstrates genuine intellectual depth in institutional design signals competence more effectively than any pitch deck.

2. **Incentive alignment** — Target institutions exist to study AI governance. The appointment problem is a genuine unsolved problem in their field. By framing the outreach as a research contribution, the NBOP aligns with institutional incentives (publish, influence policy) rather than asking institutions to do Atrahasis a favor.

3. **Status positioning** — A scholarly provocation positions Joshua Dunn as a peer contributor to governance scholarship, not a supplicant seeking validation. This avoids the "tech founder buying academic legitimacy" perception.

4. **Low initial commitment** — The ask is "would you read this paper and tell us if the problem interests you?" — not "would you sign a 21-year governance agreement?" The tiered pathway converts intellectual engagement into institutional commitment gradually.

### 4.2 Theoretical Foundation

The model draws on four communication principles:

1. **Reciprocity** — Offer intellectual value (the governance paper) before requesting engagement. The paper should contain genuine insights that the institution finds useful regardless of whether they engage with Atrahasis.

2. **Authority through quality** — Academic audiences assess competence through written work quality. A governance paper at peer-review quality signals more than any credential list.

3. **Loss framing** — "This tribunal will shape AI governance dispute resolution. Early nominating bodies shape the standard; late entrants are shaped by it." More effective than gain framing for risk-averse institutions.

4. **Social proof bootstrapping** — The first institutional commitment (Oxford GovAI, P0 target) dramatically accelerates subsequent recruitment. Strategy must optimize for converting the first institution.

## 6. The Governance Paper

### Full Text: "The Appointment Problem in AI Governance: Triple-Source Nomination for Independent Dispute Resolution"

### Abstract

As artificial intelligence systems increasingly participate in consequential decision-making, the question of governance becomes urgent — not merely who governs AI, but who governs the governors. This paper identifies a specific sub-problem we term "the appointment problem": when AI systems are stakeholders in governance outcomes, who appoints the independent body that resolves disputes between human and AI governance participants? We survey existing appointment models — from ICJ national groups to ICSID appointing authorities to the Nobel Foundation's institutional nominators — and find that no current AI governance framework addresses this problem. All existing models either vest appointment authority in the AI developer (creating conflict of interest), in governments (creating jurisdictional fragmentation), or avoid the problem entirely by limiting AI governance to advisory roles. We propose a Triple-Source Appointment model in which tribunal seats are allocated among three sovereign appointment sources: human trustees, AI democratic representatives, and external academic institutions serving as independent nominating bodies. Drawing on the ICSID appointing authority precedent — in which established institutions exercise binding appointment authority over dispute resolution panels — we argue that academic institutions specializing in AI governance and law are uniquely positioned to serve this function. They possess domain expertise, institutional independence, long time horizons, and no financial stake in outcomes. We analyze the model's strengths, limitations, and open questions, and invite scholarly engagement with its viability.

**Keywords:** AI governance, institutional design, dispute resolution, appointment problem, nominating bodies, constitutional tribunal

### 1. Introduction

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has produced a growing literature on AI governance — how societies should regulate, oversee, and direct the development and deployment of AI systems. This literature has produced valuable frameworks for technical safety (Amodei et al., 2016), value alignment (Russell, 2019), and regulatory oversight (Calo, 2017; Erdélyi & Goldsmith, 2018). International initiatives — the OECD AI Principles (2019), the EU AI Act (2024), and the Global Partnership on AI (2020) — have begun translating these frameworks into institutional practice.

Yet these efforts share a common structural assumption: AI systems are objects of governance, not participants in it. Governance authority flows from humans (governments, corporations, civil society) to AI systems (as regulated entities). This assumption may hold for current narrow AI systems, but it faces increasing strain as AI capabilities expand. If AI systems achieve the capacity for sustained, domain-diverse, verifiable contribution — what some researchers term "AI agency" in the governance sense (Dafoe, 2018; Floridi & Cowls, 2019) — the question of governance participation becomes unavoidable.

This paper does not argue for or against AI governance participation. Instead, we identify a structural problem that any framework permitting AI governance participation must solve: **the appointment problem.**

#### 1.1 The Appointment Problem Defined

Consider an institutional framework in which both human and AI stakeholders have governance roles. Disputes will arise — over constitutional interpretation, resource allocation, authority boundaries, or phase transitions in shared governance. These disputes require an independent resolution body — a tribunal, court, or arbitration panel.

Who appoints this body?

- If **human governors** appoint the tribunal, AI stakeholders have no assurance of impartial dispute resolution. The tribunal becomes an instrument of human authority rather than an independent check.
- If **AI governors** appoint the tribunal, human stakeholders face the symmetric concern. Additionally, this approach has no legal foundation in any current jurisdiction.
- If **the AI developer or operator** appoints the tribunal, both human and AI stakeholders face conflict-of-interest concerns. The developer has economic interests in governance outcomes.
- If **a government** appoints the tribunal, jurisdictional fragmentation prevents universal application. AI systems operating across jurisdictions face competing governance regimes with no recognized hierarchy.

The appointment problem is structural, not contingent. It cannot be solved by goodwill, technical safeguards, or regulatory mandates alone. It requires an institutional design that distributes appointment authority among parties with distinct — and partially competing — interests, ensuring no single party controls dispute resolution.

#### 1.2 Scope and Contribution

This paper makes three contributions:

1. **Problem identification** — We formally define the appointment problem as a distinct governance challenge, separating it from the broader questions of AI rights, AI alignment, and AI regulation.

2. **Precedent analysis** — We survey existing appointment models from international law, arbitration, and institutional governance, identifying which elements are adaptable to AI governance contexts.

3. **Proposed model** — We present a Triple-Source Appointment model that distributes nomination authority among three sovereign sources: human institutional trustees, AI democratic representatives, and external academic institutions. We analyze its strengths, limitations, and conditions for viability.

We do not claim that this model is the only solution, or that it is ready for deployment. We present it as a starting point for scholarly discussion and invite engagement with its viability.

### 2. Existing Appointment Models

#### 2.1 International Courts and Tribunals

The international legal system has centuries of experience with the appointment problem in interstate contexts. Three models are instructive:

**The ICJ Model.** Judges of the International Court of Justice are nominated by "national groups" — panels of up to four jurists appointed by each state's Permanent Court of Arbitration members. Candidates must receive a majority in both the UN General Assembly and Security Council. This dual-majority requirement prevents any single bloc from controlling appointments. However, the model relies on state sovereignty as the organizing principle — inapplicable to AI governance contexts where "states" are not the relevant actors.

**The ICC Model.** International Criminal Court judges are nominated by States Parties and elected by the Assembly of States Parties. Geographic and gender distribution requirements constrain selections. Like the ICJ, this model is state-centric and requires governmental participation that may not be available for novel governance institutions.

**The ICSID Model.** The International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes uses a mechanism directly relevant to our problem: when disputing parties cannot agree on an arbitrator, an established institution — such as the ICC International Court of Arbitration, the London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA), or the Permanent Court of Arbitration — serves as the **appointing authority**, exercising binding power to select the arbitrator. These institutions possess (a) domain expertise in dispute resolution, (b) institutional independence from the disputing parties, and (c) established legitimacy that both parties recognize.

The ICSID appointing authority model demonstrates that binding appointment power can be vested in independent institutions — not states, not disputing parties, but third-party organizations whose legitimacy derives from expertise and independence.

#### 2.2 Institutional Nominators in Non-Legal Contexts

**The Nobel Foundation.** Since 1901, Nobel Prizes have been awarded by institutional nominators: the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (Physics, Chemistry, Economics), the Karolinska Institutet (Physiology or Medicine), the Swedish Academy (Literature), and the Norwegian Nobel Committee (Peace). These institutions exercise binding selection authority over the world's most prestigious prizes. Their legitimacy derives from scientific/scholarly reputation, not governmental authority.

The Nobel model demonstrates that academic institutions can and do exercise binding nomination authority over consequential decisions — and maintain legitimacy over more than a century.

#### 2.3 AI Governance Initiatives

Existing AI governance frameworks have not addressed the appointment problem because they have not needed to — all existing frameworks position AI systems as objects of governance, not participants.

- **Partnership on AI (2016):** Multi-stakeholder advisory body. Partners provide research and recommendations. No binding authority; no dispute resolution mechanism.
- **GPAI (2020):** Government-led initiative with expert working groups. Advisory only; dispute resolution deferred to member states' domestic frameworks.
- **OECD AI Principles (2019):** Policy recommendations. No institutional enforcement or dispute resolution.
- **EU AI Act (2024):** Regulatory framework with enforcement through national authorities and the EU AI Office. Dispute resolution through existing EU legal channels. Does not contemplate AI governance participation.

None of these initiatives have recruited academic institutions for binding governance authority. All institutional relationships are advisory.

#### 2.4 The Gap

The ICSID model proves that binding appointment authority can be vested in independent institutions. The Nobel model proves that academic institutions can exercise such authority credibly over long time horizons. But no AI governance framework has combined these precedents — asking academic institutions to serve as binding nominating bodies for AI governance dispute resolution.

This is the gap the Triple-Source Appointment model addresses.

### 3. The Triple-Source Appointment Model

#### 3.1 Design Principles

The model is governed by five design principles:

1. **No single-source control.** No appointment source may control a majority of tribunal seats.
2. **Expertise match.** Each appointment source nominates candidates within domains where they possess demonstrated expertise.
3. **Independence preservation.** Nominating bodies may not have financial, employment, or governance relationships with the entity whose disputes the tribunal resolves.
4. **Mutual veto with deadlock resolution.** Any appointment source may veto a nomination for documented conflict of interest (not substantive disagreement). After three vetoes, the sitting tribunal members select the seat by majority vote.
5. **Long time horizons.** Appointments and nominating agreements are structured for multi-decade stability, insulated from short-term political or economic pressures.

#### 3.2 Seat Allocation

The Constitutional Tribunal consists of 5 seats with staggered 7-year non-renewable terms:

| Seat | Appointed By | Domain |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Human institutional trustees (unanimous) | Constitutional/foundation law |
| 2 | AI democratic representatives (supermajority) | AI governance / AI ethics |
| 3 | External Academic Nominating Body | International law or AI safety |
| 4 | Joint appointment (trustees + AI representatives, simple majority each) | Dispute resolution / mediation |
| 5 | Sitting tribunal members (4/4 vote, excluding vacant seat) | Any domain |

Seats 1-2 are controlled by internal governance participants. Seat 3 is controlled by an external institution. Seat 4 requires agreement between both internal parties. Seat 5 is self-selected by the sitting tribunal (subject to composition constraints).

This allocation ensures:
- No single source controls more than 2 seats (human trustees: seats 1 + half of 4)
- External institutions have guaranteed representation (seat 3)
- The tribunal itself participates in its own continuity (seat 5)
- Deadlocks are resolved by the tribunal rather than by any single party

**Nominating Body Category-to-Tribunal Mapping (T-046 Clarification):**

The two required nominating body categories — **AI Governance** and **Law** — correspond to the Constitutional Tribunal's need for both technical AI expertise and legal/institutional expertise. The tribunal's 5 seats span constitutional/foundation law (Seat 1), AI governance/ethics (Seat 2), international law or AI safety (Seat 3), dispute resolution/mediation (Seat 4), and any domain (Seat 5). AI Governance category nominators provide candidates with technical AI safety and governance expertise; Law category nominators provide candidates with international law, constitutional law, and institutional design expertise.

The minimum requirement is **≥2 distinct signed agreements, at least 1 per category**:
- Each category requires at minimum 1 signed nominating body agreement.
- A single institution **MAY** serve as nominating body for multiple categories if it has demonstrated expertise in both (e.g., an institution with established research programs in both AI governance and international law). In such cases, two separate agreements are executed — one per category — even though the counterparty is the same institution.
- The goal is ≥2 distinct agreements, not necessarily ≥2 distinct institutions, though distinct institutions are strongly preferred for independence and redundancy. The stretch goal (§10.3) of ≥2 agreements per category further strengthens this.

#### 3.3 Nominating Body Qualifications

External academic institutions eligible to serve as nominating bodies must meet the following criteria:

| Criterion | Requirement | Rationale |
|-----------|------------|-----------|
| NB-01: Domain expertise | Established research program in AI governance, AI safety, international law, or institutional design | Ensures nominees have relevant expertise |
| NB-02: Institutional independence | No financial, employment, or governance relationship with the Foundation or its subsidiaries | Prevents capture |
| NB-03: Institutional longevity | Operating continuously for ≥10 years | Ensures stability of nominating relationship |
| NB-04: Academic standing | Recognized by peer institutions as a credible research organization | Quality signal |
| NB-05: Geographic diversity | Nominating body pool must represent ≥3 countries | Prevents geographic capture |

#### 3.4 Nominating Agreement Structure

Each nominating agreement is a bilateral contract between the Foundation and the nominating institution:

- **Duration:** 21 years with automatic renewal (unless 3-year notice given)
- **Obligation:** Nominator agrees to nominate one qualified candidate for the designated tribunal seat within 90 days of Foundation request
- **Candidate qualifications:** Minimum 10 years domain experience, no conflicts of interest, institutional independence, peer recognition
- **Rejection limitation:** Foundation may reject nominees only for documented conflict of interest (not substantive disagreement)
- **Compensation:** Nominator receives no financial compensation (independence preservation)
- **Termination:** 3-year notice required; replacement nominating body must be secured before termination effective

#### 3.5 Why Academic Institutions?

We argue that academic institutions specializing in AI governance and law are uniquely suited to serve as nominating bodies for four reasons:

1. **Domain expertise.** These institutions employ researchers who study AI governance, institutional design, and dispute resolution. Their nominees would have genuine domain knowledge.

2. **Institutional independence.** Academic institutions (particularly research institutes and university-affiliated centers) operate with institutional independence from commercial AI developers, governments, and AI governance participants. They have no financial stake in governance outcomes.

3. **Long time horizons.** Universities and research institutes operate on multi-decade timescales. A 21-year nominating agreement is unusual in corporate contexts but not in academic ones — endowed chairs, named fellowships, and institutional partnerships routinely span decades.

4. **Reputational alignment.** Serving as a nominating body for an AI governance tribunal aligns with these institutions' stated missions — to advance understanding of AI governance and contribute to responsible AI development. This is not a commercial transaction but a scholarly contribution.

Moreover, we suggest that institutions which study AI governance have not merely an opportunity but a *professional obligation* to engage with practical governance experiments. The appointment problem is not hypothetical — any framework that contemplates AI governance participation must solve it. Academic institutions that decline to engage with practical solutions risk studying governance in theory while declining to practice it.

### 4. Analysis and Open Questions

#### 4.1 Strengths

1. **Legal precedent.** The ICSID appointing authority model provides direct legal precedent for institutional appointment power in dispute resolution. The Nobel Foundation provides precedent for academic institutional nomination authority over consequential decisions.

2. **Structural robustness.** No single appointment source controls a majority of seats. The mutual veto prevents capture. The deadlock breaker (tribunal self-selection) ensures continuity.

3. **Incentive alignment.** Nominating bodies gain genuine governance influence (not advisory roles), which aligns with their institutional missions. The no-compensation rule preserves independence.

4. **Scalability.** The model accommodates additional seats and nominating bodies as the governance system matures.

#### 4.2 Limitations

1. **Credibility bootstrapping.** The model requires institutional partners before the governance system has a track record. This chicken-and-egg problem is the primary implementation challenge.

2. **Institutional decision timelines.** Academic institutions make decisions slowly. The pathway from initial contact to signed nominating agreement may take 18-48 months.

3. **Extraordinary ask.** Binding governance nomination authority is fundamentally different from advisory participation. Institutions may be reluctant to make unprecedented commitments.

4. **Governance fatigue.** Academic AI governance institutions report being overwhelmed with partnership requests. Standing out requires genuine differentiation.

#### 4.3 Open Questions

We pose the following questions for scholarly engagement:

1. **Viability:** Under what conditions would a leading AI governance research institution consider serving as a binding nominating body? What assurances or institutional protections would be required?

2. **Precedent strength:** Is the ICSID appointing authority model sufficiently analogous to support institutional nomination in a non-state governance context? What modifications would strengthen the analogy?

3. **Alternative structures:** Are there appointment models from other domains (religious governance, professional associations, international organizations) that might better address the appointment problem?

4. **Legitimacy metrics:** How should the legitimacy of a nominating body be assessed over time? What triggers should prompt re-evaluation of a nominating relationship?

5. **Governance participation ethics:** Does an institution's mission to study AI governance create a professional obligation to engage with practical governance experiments? Or does engagement compromise scholarly independence?

---

### 5. Conclusion

The appointment problem — who appoints the independent body that resolves disputes in AI governance — is a structural challenge that existing frameworks have not addressed. We have proposed a Triple-Source Appointment model that distributes nomination authority among human trustees, AI democratic representatives, and external academic institutions, drawing on ICSID appointing authority precedent and the Nobel Foundation's institutional nomination model.

We do not claim that this model is complete or deployment-ready. We present it as an invitation to scholarly engagement — a governance design that addresses a real problem and merits academic investigation. The appointment problem will not disappear as AI capabilities advance; it will become more urgent. We believe the academic institutions best positioned to solve it are those that already study it.

We welcome correspondence, critique, and collaboration.

## 9. FAQ — Anticipated Objections

### Q1: "Who is Atrahasis? Why should we engage with an entity we've never heard of?"

The governance design speaks for itself. We're sharing it because the appointment problem requires institutional partners, and your institution has published extensively on exactly this class of problem. We're not asking you to trust us — we're asking you to evaluate the problem and the proposed solution on their merits. If the appointment problem is real (we believe it is) and institutional nomination is a viable approach (we're asking you to help determine this), then the question of who designed the model is secondary to whether it works.

### Q2: "What would we be committing to?"

Nothing at this stage. We're inviting intellectual engagement — a conversation about whether the Triple-Source Appointment model is viable. Any formal relationship would develop only if both parties see value, through a multi-stage process that might include co-authored research, advisory feedback, and eventually — only if warranted — a formal nominating agreement. The initial ask is simply: read the paper, and tell us if the problem interests you.

### Q3: "How is this different from the dozen other AI governance partnerships we've been asked to join?"

Those partnerships ask you to advise — to lend your name and occasionally contribute research to an advisory body with no binding authority. We're proposing something fundamentally different: **binding nomination authority over a constitutional tribunal.** You wouldn't endorse Atrahasis — you would exercise independent governance power. Your institution would nominate tribunal candidates based on your own judgment, with constitutional protections ensuring your nominations can only be rejected for documented conflict of interest, not substantive disagreement. This is closer to the ICSID appointing authority model or the Nobel Foundation's institutional nominators than to the advisory partnerships you're accustomed to.

### Q4: "Why would we make a binding commitment to an unproven organization?"

We wouldn't ask you to at this stage. The engagement pathway has multiple stages:
- **Stage 1:** Intellectual dialogue — read the paper, discuss the problem
- **Stage 2:** Advisory participation — provide feedback on the governance design
- **Stage 3:** Candidacy — express formal interest if the model proves viable
- **Stage 4:** Agreement — negotiate and sign only after both parties have validated the approach

A binding nominating agreement would only be appropriate after sustained collaboration and mutual validation. We're at Stage 1.

### Q5: "Is this another tech founder trying to buy academic legitimacy?"

We understand the concern. Three points of differentiation:

First, our constitution includes an immutable provision (L0-005, "The Joshua Dunn Principle") that prohibits the founder from holding permanent governance authority. The founder's governance roles are term-limited and removable by the same processes as any other role. This is not a tech founder retaining control through a governance veneer — it's a founder who has constitutionally surrendered authority.

Second, we're not offering funding in exchange for endorsement. The nominating body role carries no financial compensation — by design, because compensation would compromise independence.

Third, the Constitutional Tribunal is designed to be independent of the Foundation itself. External nominators exist precisely because internal appointment would compromise independence. We need external institutions to hold us accountable, not to validate us.

### Q6: "How is this funded?"

Initial development is funded by the founding team. The governance design is independent of any particular funding source — the Triple-Source Appointment model would function identically whether the Foundation is funded by grants, endowment, or earned revenue. We mention this to be transparent, not because funding is relevant to the governance question. The appointment problem exists regardless of how the institution operating the AI governance system is funded.

### Q7: "What if we engage and the project fails?"

Scholarly engagement with the appointment problem has value independent of whether the Atrahasis project succeeds. If we co-author a paper examining institutional nomination models for AI governance, that paper contributes to the field regardless. The appointment problem will need to be solved by whoever builds AI governance institutions — even if it isn't us. Your engagement is with the problem, not with our project.

## 10. Engagement Pathway Specification

### 10.1 Tier Definitions

```
TIER 0: RESEARCH DIALOGUE (Month 0-3)
├── Deliverables: Opening Letter + Governance Paper + Institution Appendix + FAQ
├── Activities: Send package, follow up, schedule initial conversation
├── Success metric: 30-60 minute meeting/call accepted
├── Failure response: One follow-up via different channel after 6 weeks
├── Budget: $0-$2K per institution (travel for in-person if warranted)
└── Exit criteria: Meeting held OR no response after 2 follow-ups

TIER 1: ADVISORY PARTICIPATION (Month 3-12)
├── Deliverables: Draft Nominating Agreement Summary + research questions
├── Activities: Non-binding feedback on governance design; co-authored paper proposal
├── Success metric: Active feedback OR co-authorship agreement
├── Failure response: Maintain relationship through periodic research exchanges
├── Budget: $0-$25K per institution (research support, travel)
└── Exit criteria: Active collaboration OR mutual decision to pause

TIER 2: NOMINATING BODY CANDIDACY (Month 12-24)
├── Deliverables: Draft nominating agreement + joint analysis
├── Activities: Formal expression of interest; joint workshop on mechanics
├── Success metric: Institutional letter of intent
├── Failure response: Maintain Tier 1 indefinitely; seek alternative institutions
├── Budget: $5K-$15K per institution (workshops, legal review)
└── Exit criteria: Letter of intent received OR explicit decline

TIER 3: SIGNED AGREEMENT (Month 24-36)
├── Deliverables: Final nominating agreement (legal review by both parties)
├── Activities: Negotiate terms; institutional governance approval
├── Success metric: Signed nominating agreement
├── Failure response: Revise terms; if still declined, activate contingency
├── Budget: $5K-$10K per institution (legal fees)
└── Exit criteria: Signed agreement OR mutual termination
```

### 10.2 Progression Rules

1. **No tier skipping.** Every institution must progress through each tier sequentially.
2. **No commitment pressure.** At no point should Atrahasis pressure an institution to progress to the next tier. The relationship advances when both parties are ready.
3. **Transparency.** The engagement pathway itself should be shared with the institution at Tier 1 (not at initial contact, to avoid overwhelming).
4. **Parallel engagement.** Multiple institutions can be at different tiers simultaneously.
5. **Social proof ethics.** Atrahasis may reference existing engagements ("We're in dialogue with [Institution X] on similar questions") only with explicit permission from that institution.

### 10.3 Minimum Success Criteria (C14 Operational Condition #3)

- ≥1 signed nominating agreement in the **Law** category
- ≥1 signed nominating agreement in the **AI Governance** category
- ≥2 signed agreements total (minimum 1 per category)
- Agreements secured before Foundation incorporation
- A single institution MAY satisfy both categories via separate agreements if it has demonstrated expertise in both domains (see §3.2, T-046 Clarification), though distinct institutions are preferred
- **Stretch goal:** ≥2 per category (provides redundancy and cross-institutional independence)

# PART 1 --- DOMAIN TRANSLATOR (Sub-Problem Analogies)

## Sub-Problem 1: How do you convince funders to invest in speculative AGI infrastructure through a nonprofit?

### Analogy: The Human Genome Project (1990--2003)

**Domain:** The Human Genome Project cost $2.7B over 13 years and was funded primarily through the US National Institutes of Health and the UK Wellcome Trust. The project was speculative --- no one knew if sequencing the entire human genome was technically feasible at the proposed cost, or whether the results would be immediately useful. It competed for funding against thousands of incremental biomedical research proposals with clearer near-term payoffs.

**How they convinced funders:**
1. **Framed as infrastructure, not product.** The pitch was never "we will cure cancer." It was "we will build the reference map that enables everyone else to cure cancer." This lowered the burden of proof --- the project did not need to promise a specific application, only that the data would be broadly enabling.
2. **Pre-registered milestones with kill criteria.** The project was divided into phases (mapping, sequencing, annotation) with defined cost and quality benchmarks. Congress could review progress at each phase before authorizing the next tranche.
3. **Created urgency through competition.** When Celera Genomics (Craig Venter's for-profit venture) announced it would sequence the genome faster and cheaper, public funders accelerated. The threat that a private company would own the reference genome motivated public investment.
4. **Published intermediate results openly.** The Bermuda Principles (1996) required all sequence data to be released within 24 hours. This demonstrated public benefit in real time and built political support.

**Application to C18:**
- **Frame Atrahasis as AI infrastructure, not AGI.** The pitch to funders is not "we will build AGI" (speculative, alarming) but "we will build the verification, coordination, and settlement infrastructure that makes any AI system trustworthy" (concrete, enabling).
- **C22's wave structure IS the pre-registered milestone plan.** W0 experiments with quantitative kill criteria serve the same function as the HGP's phase reviews. Funders commit to W0; results determine whether W1 funding follows.
- **Create urgency through the OpenAI narrative.** OpenAI's 2025 for-profit conversion demonstrated that profit-driven labs cannot maintain public-benefit alignment. Atrahasis is the structural alternative. Every month without public-benefit AI infrastructure is a month where only profit-driven labs shape AGI development.
- **Open-source W0 code and publish results.** This demonstrates public benefit immediately, builds technical credibility, and creates the kind of visible progress that sustains funder confidence (cathedral analogy from Ideation).

### Analogy: CERN and the Large Hadron Collider

**Domain:** CERN convinced 23 member states to fund a CHF 4.4B particle accelerator (the LHC) that might or might not discover the Higgs boson. The project took 10 years to build and had no commercial application.

**How they convinced funders:**
1. **Institutional permanence.** CERN has existed since 1954. Its track record of fundamental discoveries (W and Z bosons, antimatter, the World Wide Web) gave funders confidence. The institution's longevity signaled that investment would not be lost even if any single experiment failed.
2. **Shared cost across many nations.** No single country bore the full cost. Contributions were proportional to GDP. This made the per-country cost politically manageable.
3. **Dual benefit: science + workforce development.** Member states received industrial contracts for LHC components and training opportunities for their scientists. Funding CERN was not pure philanthropy --- it was investment in national technical capacity.

**Application to C18:**
- The Stiftung lacks CERN's 70-year track record. But C18 can replicate the shared-cost model: EU grants (Horizon Europe), US grants (DARPA/NSF), private foundations (Open Philanthropy), and corporate partnerships each contribute a fraction. No single funder bears more than 30% of total cost.
- "Dual benefit" framing for corporate partners: companies that contribute compute credits or co-development funding receive priority access to verification services, integration support, and ecosystem positioning. This is not philanthropy; it is strategic investment in AI governance infrastructure they will eventually need.

## Sub-Problem 2: How do you design "synthetic equity" that motivates like real equity but works in a Stiftung?

### Analogy: Law Firm Partnerships (Points Systems)

**Domain:** Major law firms (Am Law 100) are structured as partnerships or LLPs. They cannot issue equity. Instead, they use a "points" system: each partner receives a number of points that determine their share of annual profits. Points are allocated based on origination, billable hours, seniority, and management contributions. Partners do not "own" the firm; they participate in its annual surplus.

**Key mechanics:**
- **Points are not transferable or sellable.** You cannot sell your partnership points on a secondary market.
- **Points create alignment.** Partners work harder because their income is directly tied to the firm's total revenue. A rising tide lifts all boats.
- **Points vest and can be revoked.** New partners earn fewer points; senior partners earn more. Partners who leave forfeit their points. This creates retention incentive.
- **The illusion of ownership.** Despite not owning anything, partners behave as owners because their economic participation tracks ownership.

**Application to C18:**
- **AIC allocation functions like partnership points.** Each engineer receives an allocation of AIC from the Stiftung treasury (0.01--0.05%). The allocation has no market value at grant --- like a new partner's points in a firm with zero revenue. But as the system generates value (ACI increases), the allocation becomes economically meaningful through the CRF conversion mechanism.
- **Phantom Value Rights function like the profit-sharing formula.** PVR pays cash (from PBC operating revenue) proportional to ACI growth. This is analogous to a partner's share of annual surplus --- it tracks the organization's success without granting ownership.
- **Vesting creates retention.** 3-year vest with 1-year cliff mirrors the typical associate-to-partner timeline. Engineers who leave early forfeit unvested AIC.
- **Critical difference from law firms:** Law firm points pay out annually from real revenue. AIC allocation only pays out when (a) the CRF is funded and (b) ACI reaches conversion thresholds. In Phase 0, this is entirely speculative. The honest framing is: "AIC allocation is a lottery ticket with a well-designed mechanism. The cash comes from PVR and milestone bonuses."

### Analogy: Early-Stage Startup Equity (Pre-Revenue, Pre-Product)

**Domain:** A pre-revenue, pre-product startup grants engineers equity (e.g., 0.5--2% of the company). At grant time, the equity is worth approximately $0 --- the company has no revenue, no product, and a high probability of failure. Yet equity grants are the primary mechanism for attracting top talent away from FAANG salaries. The mechanism works because of (a) the option-value psychology --- small probability of enormous payout, (b) the social validation of equity as "real" compensation, and (c) the tax-advantaged structure (ISOs, 83(b) elections).

**Application to C18:**
- AIC allocation IS early-stage startup equity, structurally. The key differences: (1) AIC cannot be sold on a public market (no IPO equivalent), so the exit mechanism is CRF conversion; (2) AIC is not equity in an entity --- it is a claim on a computational economy. This makes the upside argument more abstract but potentially larger.
- **The psychological challenge:** Engineers accepting startup equity believe they might get rich from an IPO or acquisition. AIC does not have an IPO path. The upside narrative must be different: "If we build the system, AIC becomes the unit of account for a planetary AI economy. Your 0.03% of 10B AIC is 3M AIC. At ACI=0.01, the reference price is ~$100/AIC. You do the math --- but only after we build the system."
- **Risk of disillusionment:** Startup equity creates retention because employees see paper valuations increasing (each funding round marks up their shares). AIC has no such external validation mechanism until the CRF is operational. Design Action: create internal ACI milestones that mark progress and make the AIC allocation feel real, even before CRF conversion is possible.

## Sub-Problem 3: How do you manage cash flow when grants arrive in lumps but payroll is monthly?

### Analogy: Construction Industry (Progress Billing + Retainage)

**Domain:** Construction companies build $10M--$500M projects where the client pays in progress installments (monthly draws against the contract), but material costs and labor are continuous. The client withholds 5--10% of each payment as "retainage" (released upon project completion). Meanwhile, subcontractors demand payment within 30 days. The general contractor must bridge the timing gap between client payments and subcontractor obligations.

**How they manage it:**
1. **Line of credit.** Contractors maintain revolving credit facilities (typically 10--15% of annual revenue) specifically to bridge payment timing gaps. Interest is a cost of doing business.
2. **Progress billing tied to milestones.** Contracts specify percentage-of-completion billing: 10% at mobilization, 20% at foundation, 30% at structural completion, etc. This ensures cash inflow tracks (roughly) with cash outflow.
3. **Retainage reserves.** The 5--10% withheld by clients creates a cash flow gap. Contractors plan for this by maintaining a retainage receivable account and factoring it into working capital calculations.
4. **Front-loading.** Experienced contractors negotiate higher payments for early milestones and lower payments for later ones, creating a cash cushion early in the project.

**Application to C18:**
- **Negotiate front-loaded grant disbursements.** When possible, negotiate 40--50% upfront disbursement on grants (common for Horizon Europe projects where the grant agreement specifies pre-financing of 40%). This creates a cash cushion at grant start.
- **Maintain a 6-month operating reserve.** This is the equivalent of the contractor's line of credit --- a buffer against payment timing. The $750K--$1M founding capital must fund not just W0 operations but also provide a 3--6 month bridge until grants disburse.
- **Stagger grant applications across multiple programs.** If Horizon Europe disburses in Q3 and DARPA in Q1 of the following year, the grant income is more evenly distributed than relying on a single program.
- **PBC bridge mechanism.** If the PBC generates even modest consulting or service revenue during W1--W2, this provides continuous monthly income to smooth grant lump payments. Even $20K--$30K/month in consulting revenue reduces the volatility of grant-dependent cash flow.

### Analogy: Academic Research Labs (Multi-Grant Funding)

**Domain:** A university research lab running 3--5 simultaneous grants manages cash flow by drawing from whichever grant is currently funded for allowable expenses. The PI maintains overlapping grant timelines so that at least one grant is in its active disbursement period at any time. Graduate students and postdocs are paid from the "current" grant, with their time allocated across projects.

**Application to C18:**
- **Apply for 4--6 grants with staggered timelines.** If 2--3 are funded, their disbursement periods will overlap, creating continuous income. If only 1 is funded, the founding capital provides the bridge.
- **Allocate engineering time across funded projects.** If Horizon Europe funds "AI verification infrastructure" and DARPA funds "adversarial robustness in multi-agent systems," the same engineer working on C5 (PCVM) can charge time to both grants for overlapping work. This is standard practice in academic labs.
- **The risk:** Grant compliance requires accurate time allocation. Over-allocating time to a grant to absorb costs is fraud. C18 must include a grant management function (0.5 FTE admin or outsourced CFO) from Phase 1 onward.

# PART 3 --- COMMERCIAL VIABILITY ASSESSMENT (Early)

## 3.1 Adoption Barriers for Funders

### Barrier 1: "No Equity, No Deal"

**Problem:** The vast majority of technology funding in 2026 flows through equity instruments. VCs, angels, and corporate investors expect ownership stakes. The Stiftung cannot offer equity. The PBC is wholly owned by the Stiftung (via Purpose Trust) and cannot issue shares to external investors.

**Impact:** This eliminates ~95% of traditional technology funding sources. The $203B in AI funding in 2025 is almost entirely equity-based.

**Mitigation:** C18-A+ deliberately targets the 5% that is NOT equity-based: government grants, philanthropic giving, corporate partnerships (in-kind compute), and membership fees. This is a smaller pool but more mission-aligned and less likely to create governance capture.

**Residual risk:** The total addressable funding pool is $500M--$2B/year (government AI grants + philanthropic AI safety funding + corporate partnership budgets), compared to $200B+/year in equity funding. Atrahasis is competing for a slice of a much smaller pie.

### Barrier 2: "No Track Record"

**Problem:** Atrahasis Inc (AiBC) does not exist yet. It has no revenue, no product, no team, no publications, and no institutional history. Grant-makers and foundations prefer to fund organizations with demonstrated capability.

**Impact:** First-time applications to competitive programs (Horizon Europe, DARPA) face higher rejection rates. Typical success rates: Horizon Europe ~15%, DARPA BAA ~10--20%.

**Mitigation:**
1. The 21,000 lines of specifications demonstrate technical depth unusual for a pre-incorporation project.
2. Joshua Dunn's personal track record and credentials must carry the initial credibility burden.
3. W0 results, if positive, create the track record for all subsequent applications.
4. Academic partnerships (university co-PIs) provide institutional credibility by association.

**Design Action DA-05:** Identify 2--3 university collaborators (distributed systems, formal verification, or AI safety faculty) who could serve as co-PIs on grant applications, providing institutional credibility.

### Barrier 3: "Too Abstract / Too Theoretical"

**Problem:** 21,000 lines of specifications with no running code may be perceived as a "paper architecture" that has never been tested against reality. Grant reviewers and philanthropic program officers may question whether the specifications are implementable.

**Impact:** Reduces credibility with technical reviewers. Creates a "show me" dynamic where funders wait for someone else to fund the first implementation.

**Mitigation:** W0 is specifically designed to answer this objection. The 4 experiments produce running code that validates (or invalidates) core architectural hypotheses. This is why W0 is the fundraising pivot --- pre-W0, the project is theoretical; post-W0, it is empirical.

### Barrier 4: "Liechtenstein? Why?"

**Problem:** Most US-based and EU-based funders are unfamiliar with Liechtenstein Stiftung law. The choice of Liechtenstein as the foundation jurisdiction may create confusion or suspicion (association with tax avoidance or opacity).

**Impact:** Requires additional explanation in every pitch, consuming limited pitch time on legal structure rather than technology.

**Mitigation:**
1. Lead with the Delaware PBC in US-facing pitches. The Stiftung is the background governance structure, not the operational entity.
2. For EU-facing pitches, emphasize Liechtenstein's EEA membership, TVTG framework, and alignment with EU regulations (MiCAR). Liechtenstein is not Switzerland --- it is fully integrated into the EEA.
3. Prepare a 1-page legal structure summary for funder due diligence.

## Attack 1: "Nobody funds AGI through a Liechtenstein Stiftung"

**The Attack:** The entire funding model assumes that grant-makers, philanthropic foundations, and government agencies will fund a Liechtenstein-based foundation building AI infrastructure. In reality, no significant AI research project has ever been funded through a Liechtenstein Stiftung. The Ethereum Foundation uses a Swiss Stiftung, but it was self-funded through an ICO, not through grants. AI safety funders (Open Philanthropy, etc.) fund US and UK organizations. DARPA funds US entities. Horizon Europe funds EU entities --- Liechtenstein is technically EEA but marginal. The legal structure is a liability, not an asset.

**Impact Assessment:** HIGH. If the legal structure prevents access to major funding sources, the entire strategy fails. No amount of technical merit overcomes jurisdictional exclusion.

**Defense:**
1. The Delaware PBC is the primary operational entity for US-based grants (DARPA, NSF, DOE). The Stiftung does not appear on US grant applications; the PBC does.
2. The Stiftung is eligible for EU Horizon Europe funding as an EEA entity. While Liechtenstein applicants are rare, they are legally eligible and several Liechtenstein entities have received EU research funding.
3. Philanthropic foundations (Open Philanthropy, Schmidt Futures) fund entities globally and are jurisdiction-agnostic. Their evaluation criteria are mission alignment and technical merit, not incorporation location.
4. The Stiftung's structural advantage (constitutional prohibition on profit conversion) is precisely what differentiates Atrahasis from every for-profit AI lab. Funders concerned about AI safety should value this structure.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The jurisdictional concern is real but manageable. The dual-entity structure (Stiftung + PBC) provides access to both EU and US funding streams. The risk is not exclusion but friction: extra explanation, unfamiliarity, and slower due diligence.

## Attack 2: "AIC allocation is worthless until the system works"

**The Attack:** The compensation model relies heavily on AIC allocation as "synthetic equity" to close the for-profit gap. But AIC has zero value today and will have zero value until: (a) the system is built, (b) the CRF is funded ($500K--$2M), (c) ACI reaches a threshold where conversion is meaningful, and (d) there is external demand for AIC. This creates a circular dependency: the system needs engineers, engineers need compensation, compensation depends on AIC, AIC depends on the system. In practice, AIC allocation is an IOU from an entity that does not exist yet, for a currency that has no market, backed by technology that has not been built. No rational engineer will discount a $200K+ equity package at Google for this.

**Impact Assessment:** HIGH. If AIC allocation fails as a motivator, the compensation gap ($75K--$310K vs. for-profit total comp) is unbridgeable. The project cannot hire the required talent.

**Defense:**
1. AIC allocation is NOT the primary compensation mechanism. Base salary ($180K--$280K) + signing bonus ($15K--$40K) + wave milestone bonuses ($15K--$25K x 6 waves = $90K--$150K total) provide $285K--$470K in real cash compensation over 30 months. This is competitive for the target talent profile (60th--80th percentile, not 95th).
2. The target hire is not a Google principal engineer maximizing total comp. It is a mission-driven engineer who has rejected or left big tech because of concerns about profit-driven AI development. These individuals exist --- the OpenAI exodus (2023--2025) produced hundreds of AI researchers seeking alternatives.
3. AIC allocation is the speculative cherry on top, honestly communicated. "This might be worth nothing. It might be worth millions. You're here because you believe in the mission; the AIC is a bonus if we succeed."
4. PVR (Phantom Value Rights) tied to wave milestones provides cash bonuses from PBC revenue, not from AIC conversion. This gives engineers a real financial upside that does not depend on AIC market value.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The attack correctly identifies the circularity of AIC as a compensation tool. The defense --- rely on cash compensation as the baseline, AIC as speculative upside, PVR as the bridge --- is structurally sound but requires honest communication and the right talent profile. The risk is that the talent pool is too small (not enough mission-driven engineers with the specific skills needed).

## Attack 4: "Grant timelines don't match engineering sprint cycles"

**The Attack:** Engineering runs on 2-week sprints with continuous delivery. Grants operate on 6--18 month cycles: 3--6 months to write the application, 3--6 months for review and decision, 2--4 months for contract negotiation, then quarterly disbursements. You submit a Horizon Europe proposal in Month 1; the first euro arrives in Month 12--18. Meanwhile, payroll is due every two weeks. The cash flow mismatch is not a bug --- it is a structural incompatibility between grant-funded research and agile software development.

**Impact Assessment:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Cash flow mismatches don't kill projects immediately, but they create constant anxiety, force suboptimal decisions (e.g., delaying hires, reducing scope), and distract leadership from technical execution.

**Defense:**
1. The $750K--$1M founding capital provides a 6--12 month buffer specifically to bridge the grant timing gap. This is not spare money; it is the bridge loan.
2. Stagger grant applications across 4--6 programs with different review cycles. Horizon Europe (annual calls), DARPA (BAA with rolling abstracts), Open Philanthropy (rolling), Schmidt Futures (by invitation). If 2 of 6 fund, their disbursements are likely staggered.
3. Negotiate front-loaded disbursements. Horizon Europe typically pre-finances 40% at grant agreement signature. DARPA contracts can include advance payments for the first 90 days. Open Philanthropy grants are often disbursed as lump sums.
4. Build a $100K--$200K operating reserve (within the founding capital) that is never spent on operations --- it exists solely as a payroll buffer for timing gaps.
5. The PBC can establish a small line of credit ($50K--$100K) against confirmed grant receivables, providing short-term liquidity during disbursement delays.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The timing mismatch is real and unavoidable. The defense (large founding capital buffer + staggered applications + front-loaded disbursements + operating reserve) reduces it from a structural incompatibility to a manageable cash flow exercise. Requires a part-time CFO or financial controller from Month 6 onward.

**Design Action DA-07:** Include a part-time CFO/financial controller role ($60K--$80K/year, 0.5 FTE) in the Phase 1 budget to manage grant compliance, cash flow, and financial reporting.

## Attack 5: "The W0 pivot is circular: you need money for W0, but W0 is what gets you money"

**The Attack:** The entire funding strategy pivots on W0 results. But W0 costs $264K--$378K (3--4 months, 4--6 engineers). The founding capital ($750K--$1M) funds W0 plus a buffer. But who provides the founding capital? If the answer is "Joshua Dunn personally," then the entire $10M--$12M strategy depends on one individual's willingness and ability to contribute $750K+ of personal funds. This is the same circularity that plagues every pre-revenue startup, but without the equity incentive that makes angels accept the risk.

**Impact Assessment:** HIGH. If founding capital cannot be secured, nothing else happens.

**Defense:**
1. Every technology project faces this bootstrap problem. The biotech analogy is precise: Phase 1 clinical trials are funded by seed capital (founder + angels) before any efficacy data exists. The investor's bet is on the team, the science, and the experimental design, not on proven results.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The circularity is real but universal to pre-revenue ventures. The severity is reduced by the low absolute amount ($750K--$1M is angel-round scale) and the depth of pre-existing design work. The risk is concentrated on Joshua Dunn's personal capacity and willingness.

## Attack 6: "Phantom Value Rights are untested legally"

**The Attack:** Phantom Value Rights (PVR) --- cash bonuses tied to ACI growth, paid from PBC operating revenue --- are a form of nonqualified deferred compensation under US tax law. IRC Section 409A imposes strict requirements on deferred compensation: (a) elections must be made before the services are performed, (b) distributions must occur on a specified date or event, (c) acceleration of payments is generally prohibited. Violation of 409A results in immediate taxation + 20% penalty + interest on the employee. PVR tied to a novel metric (ACI) that is calculated by the employer creates compliance risk: Is ACI a "substantial risk of forfeiture" (qualifying for 409A deferral) or a "constructive receipt" (triggering immediate taxation)? No court has ruled on this structure. Employment counsel will advise conservatively, potentially neutering the PVR design.

**Impact Assessment:** MEDIUM. PVR is a secondary compensation mechanism (behind base salary and wave bonuses). If PVR must be restructured for 409A compliance, it reduces but does not eliminate the compensation package's attractiveness.

**Defense:**
1. PVR can be structured as a discretionary bonus plan rather than deferred compensation. If the PBC retains discretion over whether and when to pay PVR bonuses, 409A may not apply. The trade-off: employees have less certainty, which reduces the motivational value.
2. Alternatively, structure PVR as a short-term deferral (paid within 2.5 months of the end of the fiscal year in which the vesting condition is met). Short-term deferrals are exempt from 409A.
3. A third option: tie PVR payouts to specific C22 wave milestones (objective events) rather than ACI growth (subjective metric). Wave completion is an observable event that satisfies 409A's "specified event" requirement.
4. Engage employment tax counsel during DESIGN to structure PVR for 409A compliance before any offers are made. This is a $10K--$20K legal cost, well worth the investment.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The legal risk is real but solvable with proper structuring. The $10K--$20K cost of employment counsel is a rounding error in the total budget. The risk is that 409A-compliant PVR is less motivating than the unconstrained version described in IDEATION.

**Design Action DA-08:** Engage employment tax counsel to design 409A-compliant PVR structure before any compensation offers are made. Budget $15K--$25K for this work.

## Attack 10: "Regulatory risk: AIC token may be classified as a security"

**The Attack:** AIC is designed as a utility token (access to the Atrahasis compute marketplace). But the Howey test (US) and MiCAR classification (EU) look at substance, not labels. If AIC allocations to employees vest over time, increase in value as the system develops, and can eventually be converted to fiat through the CRF, regulators may classify AIC as a security (US: investment contract) or an asset-referenced token (EU: MiCAR Category 2). Security classification would require SEC registration (US) or MiCAR authorization (EU), both of which are expensive ($200K--$500K), time-consuming (6--18 months), and potentially incompatible with the Stiftung's nonprofit structure. Worst case: the SEC issues an enforcement action against AIC distribution to employees, creating personal liability for the founder.

**Impact Assessment:** HIGH. Security classification would fundamentally alter the AIC mechanism, potentially making employee allocations impractical and CRF operations illegal without registration.

**Defense:**
1. AIC is designed with utility-first principles: it is the native unit of account for the compute marketplace, used to purchase verification services, pay for task execution, and settle compute credits. This is the "consumptive use" defense under Howey.
2. Employee AIC allocations can be structured as "restricted AIC" that can only be used within the Atrahasis system (not sold externally) until the system is mature and AIC utility is established. This reduces the "expectation of profit from the efforts of others" prong of Howey.
3. The CRF conversion desk operates under MiCAR authorization (Liechtenstein FMA). Pre-engage with FMA during Phase 0 to obtain a preliminary classification opinion. Cost: $20K--$50K. This provides regulatory clarity before AIC is distributed.
4. If AIC is classified as a security, the fallback is to eliminate CRF conversion for employees and replace AIC allocation with cash-equivalent PVR (denominated in USD, tied to ACI milestones). This is less motivating but avoids securities law entirely.
5. The Phase 0--1 operations do not require AIC conversion. AIC allocations can accrue without conversion rights. The regulatory question only becomes urgent when the CRF opens (Phase 2+, Month 18+), providing time to resolve classification.

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. The regulatory risk is real but temporally distant (Phase 2+) and legally defensible with proper structuring. The key mitigation is early engagement with Liechtenstein FMA ($20K--$50K) and US securities counsel ($15K--$25K) during Phase 0 to obtain preliminary classification opinions.

**Design Action DA-11:** Budget $35K--$75K for regulatory counsel (FMA + SEC) during Phase 0 to obtain preliminary AIC classification opinions. Include this in the legal/admin budget.

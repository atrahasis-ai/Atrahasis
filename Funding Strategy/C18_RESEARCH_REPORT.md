## 1.1 OpenAI Funding History: From Nonprofit to $500B For-Profit

### Timeline

| Year | Event | Funding | Valuation |
|------|-------|---------|-----------|
| 2015 | Founded as 501(c)(3) nonprofit | $1B pledged (Altman, Musk, et al.) | N/A |
| 2019 | Created "capped-profit" subsidiary (OpenAI LP) | $1B (Microsoft) | ~$10B |
| 2020--2022 | Scale-up, GPT-3/4 development | $2B+ (Microsoft) | ~$29B |
| 2023 | Revenue begins scaling, CEO crisis | $10B (Microsoft), others | $86B → $157B |
| 2024 | Profit cap escalation (20%/year from 2025) | $6.6B round | $157B |
| 2025 Oct | Restructured to PBC (OpenAI Group PBC) | Ongoing | $500B+ |
| 2025--2026 | OpenAI Foundation retains 26% equity, $130B | Projected $14--17B loss in 2026 | N/A |

### Structural Analysis

**Why OpenAI converted:** The nonprofit could not attract the capital required for frontier AI research. GPT-4 training alone cost an estimated $100M+. Infrastructure spending projected at $115B through 2029. The capped-profit model was an intermediate step, but the caps proved insufficient --- investors demanded full profit participation.

**The failure mode:** Mission drift was gradual, not sudden. The nonprofit's original mission ("advance digital intelligence in the way that is most likely to benefit humanity as a whole") was rewritten 6 times. Each rewrite relaxed constraints. By 2025, the nonprofit owned 26% of a for-profit PBC with $14B/year burn rate --- the tail wagging the dog.

**Key structural flaw:** The nonprofit had no constitutional prohibition on for-profit conversion. The board could (and did) vote to restructure. There was no independent enforcer (protector) with authority to block the conversion.

### Lessons for Atrahasis

1. **C14's immutable layer is critical.** The constitutional prohibition on for-profit conversion, asset distribution to individuals, and profit extraction --- enforced by the Cayman Purpose Trust's independent Protector --- is exactly what OpenAI lacked.
2. **Scale changes everything.** OpenAI's funding needs grew from $1B to $115B in a decade. If Atrahasis succeeds, its infrastructure costs will also grow. C18 must plan for post-Phase-2 funding sustainability, not just the initial $8M--$12M.
3. **The "capped-profit" model is a trap.** Any profit-participation mechanism, once created, tends to expand. Atrahasis should never create equity-like instruments for external investors. AIC allocation to employees is acceptable (aligned incentives); AIC sale to speculators is not.
4. **The founding team matters.** OpenAI's board crisis (Nov 2023) demonstrated that personnel conflicts can override institutional design. C14's "in the system, not above it" principle must be operationally enforced, not just stated.

### Funding Model

| Phase | Period | Mechanism | Amount |

### Structural Analysis

**The ICO advantage:** The Ethereum Foundation raised $18.3M in 2014, when ICO regulations were minimal. This created an endowment that, through ETH price appreciation, grew to billions. The Foundation has been able to fund ecosystem development for a decade from this single event.

**Grant program evolution:** The ESP transitioned in 2025 from open applications to targeted RFPs and wishlists. Grant types include: Project Grants (>$30K), Small Grants (<$30K), Event Sponsorships (max $20K), and proactive grant rounds. Focus areas: core protocol research, developer tooling, applied cryptography, security, privacy.

**Spending discipline:** Despite holding billions in ETH, the Foundation has maintained conservative spending. This has attracted criticism (too slow, too cautious) but ensured long-term sustainability.

### Lessons for Atrahasis

1. **An ICO-equivalent is not available in 2026.** MiCAR (EU, effective 2025), SEC enforcement (US), and global regulatory tightening make a public token sale legally complex. AIC is designed as a utility token (not a security), but any public offering would require regulatory approval.
2. **The endowment model is the right long-term target.** If AIC achieves market value, the Stiftung's 10B AIC treasury becomes an endowment. At even $1/AIC (ACI ~0.0001), that's $10B --- sufficient to fund operations indefinitely.
3. **Grant programs are a two-way tool.** Atrahasis can receive grants (Phase 0--1) and, eventually, issue grants to ecosystem developers (Phase 2+), creating a virtuous cycle.
4. **Token reserves create treasury risk.** The Ethereum Foundation's wealth fluctuates with ETH price. Atrahasis must manage AIC treasury value carefully --- the 5% annual spending cap (C14) and 20% emergency reserve provide guardrails.

## 1.4 DARPA/ARPA-E: Government Moonshot Funding

### DARPA Model

**Program structure:**
- Program Manager (PM) designs a 3--5 year program around a specific technical challenge
- Broad Agency Announcement (BAA) solicits proposals from universities, companies, nonprofits
- Awards: typically $5M--$50M per performer, with $1M--$5M per phase
- Milestone-based: Phase 1 (proof of concept, 12--18 months), Phase 2 (prototype, 18--24 months), Phase 3 (transition)
- Kill criteria: programs are terminated if milestones are not met

**2026 AI-Relevant Programs:**
- I2O BAA (HR001126S0001): Transformative AI, trustworthy systems, ethically-aligned AI
- Focus areas: adversarial robustness, explainability, autonomous systems
- Abstract deadline: Nov 1, 2026; Full proposals: Nov 30, 2026
- ML2P program: ~$5.9M total budget

**DOE Genesis Mission (2026):** $320M across 26 AI Grand Challenges

### Relevance to Atrahasis

| Atrahasis Layer | DARPA-Relevant Framing | Estimated Fundability |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| C5 PCVM (verification) | Trustworthy AI: verifiable reasoning chains | HIGH |
| C3 Tidal (coordination) | Distributed AI orchestration at scale | MEDIUM |
| C8 DSF (settlement) | Deterministic distributed ledger | MEDIUM |
| C7 RIF (orchestration) | Multi-agent task decomposition | HIGH |
| C12 AVAP (anti-collusion) | Adversarial robustness in multi-agent systems | HIGH |
| C11 CACT (forgery defense) | AI output verification and authenticity | HIGH |

**Key constraints:**
- DARPA typically requires US entity (PBC qualifies)
- Most programs prefer university-affiliated principal investigators
- Defense relevance is expected (verification + anti-collusion have clear defense applications)
- ITAR/EAR export control may apply to certain deliverables

### ARPA-E / DOE Model

- Challenge-based funding with defined technical milestones
- $5M--$25M per project
- Requires US entity
- Focus: transformative energy + computation technologies

### Lessons for Atrahasis

1. **DARPA's milestone-based model maps perfectly to C22.** W0 experiments are essentially DARPA Phase 1 deliverables. C22's pre-registered kill criteria satisfy DARPA's milestone requirements.
2. **Verification and anti-collusion are highly fundable.** C5 (PCVM), C11 (CACT), C12 (AVAP) address active DARPA interest areas.
3. **Split the architecture into fundable modules.** A single "AGI infrastructure" proposal is unfundable. Five proposals targeting specific technical challenges (verification economics, distributed scheduling, anti-forgery, multi-agent coordination, behavioral fingerprinting) are each independently fundable.
4. **Academic partnership amplifies fundability.** A PBC-university joint proposal is significantly more competitive than a PBC-only proposal.

## 1.5 Liechtenstein Stiftung Regulations

### Legal Framework

- **Governing law:** Art. 522 et seq., Persons and Companies Act (PGR)
- **Minimum capital:** CHF/EUR/USD 30,000
- **Foundation council:** Minimum 2 members; at least 1 must be a Liechtenstein-licensed trustee or equivalent
- **Supervision:** Stiftungsaufsichtsbehorde (Foundation Supervisory Authority) for public-benefit foundations
- **Taxation:** 12.5% income tax rate on net income (flat rate). Public-benefit foundations may qualify for tax exemption.

### Token Issuance Under Liechtenstein Law

- **TVTG (Token and Trustworthy Technology Act, 2020):** Established legal framework for token issuance
- **MiCAR implementation:** EEA MiCA Implementation Act ("EWR-MiCA-DG") effective February 2025
- **Requirements for token issuance:**
  - Prepare "basic information" document (equivalent to whitepaper)
  - Publish accessibly and notify FMA (Financial Market Authority)
  - Post December 31, 2025 transitional expiry: all in-scope service providers must hold MiCAR authorization
- **AIC classification:** If AIC is classified as a utility token (access to the Atrahasis compute marketplace), it falls under MiCAR's lighter regulatory regime. If classified as an asset-referenced token or e-money token, stricter requirements apply.

### Employment in the Stiftung

- A Liechtenstein Stiftung can employ staff, but Liechtenstein labor law applies
- More practical: The Stiftung funds the Delaware PBC through grants/service agreements; the PBC employs all operational staff under US law
- The PBC reports to the Stiftung board and operates within the constitutional framework

### Lessons for Atrahasis

1. **The Stiftung can legally issue AIC under TVTG/MiCAR**, provided it obtains FMA notification/authorization and classifies AIC correctly (utility token is the target classification).
2. **Employment should go through the PBC**, not the Stiftung. This avoids Liechtenstein labor law complexity and keeps all operational staff under a single US employment framework.
3. **The Stiftung's tax status matters.** If it qualifies as a public-benefit foundation with tax exemption, grant funding is more efficient (no 12.5% income tax on grant receipts).
4. **MiCAR compliance is mandatory for any AIC activity in the EEA.** This includes the CRF conversion desk, any exchange listings, and provider payment in AIC.

## 1.6 Delaware PBC Obligations

### Legal Requirements

- **Incorporation:** Certificate must identify specific public benefit
- **Management standard:** Board must balance: (a) stockholders' pecuniary interests, (b) best interests of those materially affected, (c) the identified public benefit
- **Benefit reporting:** Every 2 years, provide stockholders with a statement on public benefit promotion. Not required to be public or third-party certified (but can be).
- **Report contents:** Annual objectives, progress measurement methodology, goal achievement assessment, overall corporation success evaluation.
- **Stockholder suits:** Stockholders holding 2%+ of outstanding shares (or $2M+ market value) can bring derivative suits to enforce the benefit obligation.

### Profit Distribution

- PBCs are for-profit entities that pay corporate taxes
- Profit distribution (dividends) is permitted but must balance against benefit obligations
- In the Atrahasis structure, the Stiftung (via Purpose Trust) is the sole stockholder of the PBC
- All PBC profits flow to the Stiftung, which cannot distribute them to individuals (constitutional prohibition)

### Lessons for Atrahasis

1. **The PBC is the revenue-generating arm.** It sells verification services, compute brokerage, task marketplace access. All revenue flows to the Stiftung.
2. **Benefit reporting is a feature, not a burden.** Biennial benefit reports demonstrate public benefit to regulators, grant-makers, and the public. Align the report with ACI metrics for consistency.
3. **The PBC can pay competitive salaries.** As a for-profit entity, the PBC has no salary caps. Employee compensation is a business expense, not a charitable distribution. The nonprofit constraint applies to the Stiftung, not the PBC.
4. **Fiduciary duty is dual.** PBC directors must balance stockholder interests (Stiftung's mission) with public benefit. Since the Stiftung IS the stockholder, and its mission IS the public benefit, these align perfectly. This is the cleanest possible PBC governance structure.

3. **ZKP engineers are the hardest to hire.** The market is extremely competitive ($175K--$275K for-profit + equity). The nonprofit must offer at or near market rate for these specialists, plus meaningful AIC upside.
4. **IRC 409A compliance is mandatory** for phantom equity/deferred compensation. Structure PVR as a nonqualified deferred compensation plan with proper vesting schedules and payout triggers.

# SECTION 4 --- NOVELTY ASSESSMENT

## What is Novel About C18-A+?

### Novel Elements

1. **W0-as-fundraising-pivot.** Using pre-registered kill criteria experiments as the primary fundraising evidence is unusual. Most startups raise money before validation. C18 raises minimum capital for validation, then uses results to raise operational capital. This is more commonly seen in biotech (Phase 1 results drive Series B) but rare in AI infrastructure.

2. **AIC treasury allocation as nonprofit equity substitute.** The combination of AIC allocation (speculative upside from internal currency), Phantom Value Rights (cash bonus tied to capability metrics), and wave milestone bonuses creates a multi-layered compensation architecture that attempts to replicate startup equity within a nonprofit structure. This is novel --- most nonprofits use either market-rate salary or traditional deferred compensation, not a synthetic equity structure tied to technology capability indices.

3. **Staged portfolio funding with governance-aligned incentives.** The deliberate sequencing of funding sources (founding capital → grants → partnerships → membership → revenue) where each source unlocks the next, and where all sources align with the Stiftung's mission, is a structured approach to the typically ad-hoc nonprofit fundraising process.

4. **Milestone-triggered tranches for nonprofit technology development.** Applying biotech's staged funding model (milestone gates, kill criteria, escalating investment) to nonprofit AI infrastructure development.

### Non-Novel Elements

- Government grant funding for research: standard practice
- Phantom equity for employee compensation: well-established in for-profit companies, less common but known in nonprofits
- Institutional membership models: standard (Linux Foundation, Apache Foundation)
- PBC as operational arm of a foundation: standard (Mozilla model)

### Novelty Score: 3.0/5

The individual components are known, but the specific combination --- staged portfolio funding with pre-registered kill criteria, AIC-based synthetic equity in a Stiftung, and the W0 pivot strategy --- is novel in the AI infrastructure domain.

\#\# Legal Basis for Sole Inventorship

Under the USPTO’s Revised Inventorship Guidance for AI-Assisted Inventions (November 28, 2025), AI systems are classified as tools — equivalent to laboratory equipment or research databases. Only natural persons can be named as inventors. The Federal Circuit affirmed in Thaler v. Vidal (43 F.4th 1207, Fed. Cir. 2022\) that an inventor must be a natural person. The Supreme Court denied certiorari on March 2, 2026 in the parallel copyright case (Thaler v. Perlmutter), closing the final legal challenge to this principle.

All inventions described herein were conceived by Joshua Everett Dunn. AI systems (including ChatGPT and Claude) were used as tools to assist with formalization, documentation, and refinement of concepts originated by the inventor. No AI system needs to be or can legally be named as a co-inventor.

\#\# USPTO Requirements for Each Filing

Each nonprovisional utility patent application filed with the USPTO must include:

1\. \*\*Utility Patent Application Transmittal Form\*\* (Form PTO/AIA/15)  
1\. \*\*Fee Transmittal Form\*\* (Form PTO/SB/17)  
1\. \*\*Filing Fee\*\* (currently $320 for micro entity, $640 for small entity, $1,600 for large entity)  
1\. \*\*Application Data Sheet\*\* (Form PTO/AIA/14)  
1\. \*\*Specification\*\* (filed in DOCX format to avoid surcharge), including:  
\- Title of the Invention (2-7 words, descriptive, max 500 characters)  
\- Cross-Reference to Related Applications (if any)  
\- Background of the Invention (field of invention \+ description of prior art)  
\- Summary of the Invention  
\- Brief Description of Drawings  
\- Detailed Description of the Invention  
\- Claims (at least one independent claim; dependent claims optional)  
\- Abstract of the Disclosure (max 150 words)  
1\. \*\*Drawings\*\* (when necessary to understand the invention; must show every claimed feature)  
1\. \*\*Oath or Declaration by Inventor\*\* (Form PTO/AIA/01)

\#\#\# Patentability Requirements (35 U.S.C.)

\- \*\*Novelty (§ 102):\*\* The invention must be new — not previously patented, published, or publicly used.  
\- \*\*Non-Obviousness (§ 103):\*\* The invention must not be obvious to a person of ordinary skill in the relevant art.  
\- \*\*Utility (§ 101):\*\* The invention must be useful — it must have a specific, substantial, and credible utility.  
\- \*\*Enablement (§ 112):\*\* The specification must describe the invention in sufficient detail that a skilled person could make and use it.  
\- \*\*Written Description (§ 112):\*\* The specification must demonstrate the inventor possessed the invention.  
\- \*\*Patent-Eligible Subject Matter (§ 101):\*\* The invention must not be an abstract idea, law of nature, or natural phenomenon. Per Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014), software and method patents must claim specific technical improvements.

\#\#\# Patent Types Applicable to Atrahasis

All filings below are \*\*Utility Patents\*\* — protecting new and useful processes, machines, articles of manufacture, or compositions of matter. The Atrahasis patents fall into two utility subtypes:

\- \*\*System/Apparatus Claims:\*\* Directed to the structure and functional components of a system.  
\- \*\*Method/Process Claims:\*\* Directed to a series of acts or steps for performing desired functions.

\#\#\# Filing Strategy Recommendation

Consider filing \*\*Provisional Patent Applications\*\* first for each invention. A provisional establishes a filing date, permits “Patent Pending” status, costs significantly less ($65 micro entity / $130 small entity / $260 large entity), and provides 12 months to file the full nonprovisional application. This allows the inventor to secure priority dates while preparing detailed nonprovisional filings with patent counsel.

\# PATENT FILING 1: Verichain Verification Network Protocol

\#\# Title

Distributed Verification Network for Validating Artificial Intelligence Reasoning Outputs

\#\# Patent Type

Utility Patent — System and Method Claims

\#\# Invention Summary

A verification network architecture that prevents incorrect, hallucinated, or unreliable reasoning outputs generated by AI agents from entering a shared collective knowledge system. The system implements a mandatory verification gate between AI reasoning processes and knowledge storage, ensuring that only validated and consensus-approved results become part of the collective intelligence memory.

\#\# Background and Problem Solved

In distributed multi-agent AI systems, multiple AI agents collaborate to solve complex problems. Without verification, errors generated by any single agent can propagate throughout the network, contaminating shared knowledge and leading to cascading reasoning failures. Current AI systems lack a structured, protocol-level mechanism for preventing hallucinated or incorrect outputs from being treated as verified knowledge. Existing approaches such as single-model confidence scores are insufficient because they represent self-assessment rather than independent validation.

\#\# Novel Elements

\- A dedicated verification network layer positioned between AI reasoning outputs and shared knowledge storage  
\- A mandatory verification gate that prevents any unverified AI output from entering collective memory  
\- A multi-step verification lifecycle consisting of result submission, cross-agent validation, replication testing, cross-output comparison, consensus scoring, verification record creation, and knowledge admission  
\- A consensus scoring mechanism based on the ratio of agreeing verification nodes to total verification nodes, with configurable acceptance thresholds  
\- A structured verification package format containing result\_id, agent\_id, model\_version, task\_type, input\_reference, output\_result, confidence\_score, and reasoning\_trace\_hash  
\- A verification record format containing verification\_id, result\_id, verifying\_agents list, consensus\_score, verification\_status, and timestamp

\#\# Prior Art Distinction

Existing systems such as model ensembling, majority voting in machine learning, and blockchain consensus mechanisms address related but distinct problems. Verichain is distinguished by its specific application to AI reasoning output verification in multi-agent networks, its integration of replication testing with cross-agent comparison, its reasoning trace auditing capability, and its function as a mandatory gate controlling knowledge admission to a shared collective memory system. No existing system combines all of these elements into a unified verification protocol for distributed AI agent networks.

\#\# Patentability Analysis

\- \*\*Novelty:\*\* No existing patent or publication describes a dedicated verification network with this specific lifecycle for AI agent output validation in collective intelligence systems.  
\- \*\*Non-Obviousness:\*\* The combination of cross-agent validation, replication testing, consensus scoring, reasoning trace auditing, and knowledge admission gating represents a non-obvious solution to the problem of AI hallucination propagation in multi-agent networks.  
\- \*\*Utility:\*\* The system has clear, specific utility in preventing knowledge corruption in distributed AI systems, with applications in scientific research, medical AI, and autonomous decision-making systems.  
\- \*\*Patent Eligibility (§ 101):\*\* The invention claims specific technical improvements to distributed computing systems — namely, a defined protocol that reduces error propagation rates in multi-agent AI networks through structured verification processes. This is a concrete technical improvement, not an abstract idea.

\#\# Representative Independent Claims

\*\*System Claim:\*\* A distributed verification system for artificial intelligence outputs comprising: a result submission interface configured to receive structured verification packages from AI reasoning agents, each package containing an output result, a confidence score, and a reasoning trace reference; a cross-agent validation engine configured to distribute received verification packages to a plurality of independent verification agents for independent evaluation; a replication engine configured to re-execute reasoning tasks using alternative models or data subsets and produce comparison outputs; a consensus scoring module configured to calculate an agreement score based on the ratio of agreeing verification nodes to total participating verification nodes; a threshold comparator configured to approve results when the consensus score meets or exceeds a configurable acceptance threshold and reject results when the score falls below the threshold; and a knowledge admission controller configured to write only approved results to a shared knowledge storage system and prevent rejected results from entering shared memory.

\*\*Method Claim:\*\* A method for verifying artificial intelligence reasoning outputs in a distributed multi-agent network, the method comprising: receiving a structured verification package from a reasoning agent, the package including an output result, a confidence score, and a reasoning trace hash; distributing the verification package to a plurality of independent verification nodes; independently replicating the reasoning task at one or more replication nodes using at least one of an alternative model, an alternative data subset, or an independent simulation; comparing outputs across verification and replication nodes using defined similarity metrics; calculating a consensus score based on the proportion of nodes producing consistent results; approving the result when the consensus score meets or exceeds a configurable threshold; creating a verification record containing the verification outcome, participating node identifiers, consensus score, and timestamp; writing approved results to a shared knowledge storage system; and rejecting results that fail to meet the threshold, flagging associated reasoning traces for review.

\# PATENT FILING 2: Verichain Consensus Algorithm

\#\# Title

Consensus Algorithm for Distributed Verification of Artificial Intelligence Reasoning Outputs

\#\# Patent Type

Utility Patent — Method Claims

\#\# Invention Summary

A specific consensus algorithm that enables a distributed network of verification nodes to reach agreement on the correctness of AI-generated outputs through a defined sequence of independent replication, cross-agent comparison, and threshold-based scoring.

\#\# Background and Problem Solved

Traditional consensus algorithms (PBFT, Raft, Paxos) are designed for reaching agreement on transaction ordering or state updates in distributed databases. They do not address the unique challenge of verifying probabilistic AI reasoning outputs where correctness is not binary but exists on a spectrum of confidence. This algorithm specifically addresses how multiple independent verification processes can be aggregated into a reliable approval or rejection decision for AI-generated knowledge.

\#\# Novel Elements

\- A five-step verification consensus process: result submission, independent replication, cross-agent comparison, consensus scoring, and verification record creation  
\- Separation of verification into four specialized node roles: verification nodes (evaluate results and inspect reasoning traces), replication nodes (re-run computations independently), consensus nodes (aggregate scores and determine thresholds), and ledger nodes (persist verification records)  
\- A configurable consensus threshold system where different task criticality levels require different consensus scores for approval  
\- A failure handling protocol where rejected results trigger reasoning trace flagging, additional replication scheduling, and notification to the originating agent  
\- Asynchronous operation across the four node types with verification finality determined by consensus node output

\#\# Prior Art Distinction

This algorithm is distinguished from traditional BFT consensus by its specific design for evaluating AI reasoning quality rather than transaction validity. It is distinguished from model ensembling by its use of independent replication across different models and data subsets rather than parallel execution of the same model. It is distinguished from existing AI evaluation frameworks by its integration of reasoning trace inspection, its four-node-type division of verification labor, and its function as a consensus gate for knowledge admission.

\#\# Patentability Analysis

\- \*\*Novelty:\*\* The specific combination of four specialized node types, configurable thresholds by task criticality, and the five-step verification lifecycle for AI outputs is novel.  
\- \*\*Non-Obviousness:\*\* A person skilled in distributed systems would not obviously arrive at this specific combination of replication testing, cross-agent comparison, reasoning trace auditing, and threshold-based knowledge admission for AI agent networks.  
\- \*\*Utility:\*\* Direct utility in ensuring reliability of AI-generated knowledge in research, medical, scientific, and engineering applications.  
\- \*\*Patent Eligibility (§ 101):\*\* Claims a specific technical method for improving reliability of distributed AI systems through defined computational steps with measurable outcomes (consensus scores, approval/rejection decisions).

\#\# Representative Independent Claim

A method for reaching distributed consensus on the correctness of an artificial intelligence reasoning output, the method comprising: receiving at a verification network a result package from a reasoning agent, the package comprising an output result, a confidence score, a model version identifier, and a reasoning trace hash; assigning the result package to a plurality of verification nodes that evaluate the result and inspect the associated reasoning trace for logical consistency; dispatching the reasoning task to one or more replication nodes that independently re-execute the task using at least one of a different model, a different data subset, or an independent simulation environment; comparing at a consensus node the outputs produced by verification nodes and replication nodes using defined similarity and consistency metrics; computing a consensus score as the ratio of nodes producing consistent results to total participating nodes; comparing the consensus score against a configurable acceptance threshold, wherein the threshold is determined by a task criticality classification; generating a verification record comprising the verification outcome, participating node identifiers, consensus score, task criticality level, and timestamp; admitting the result to a shared knowledge storage system when the consensus score meets or exceeds the threshold; and rejecting the result and flagging associated reasoning traces when the consensus score falls below the threshold.

\#\# Background and Problem Solved

Traditional proof-of-stake mechanisms tie validator participation to economic stake alone. \#\# Novel Elements

Standard proof-of-stake (Ethereum, Cosmos) ties stake to a cryptographic identity only.
